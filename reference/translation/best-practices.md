# Translation Best Practices for Fiction

## Core Principle

Translation is not word-for-word conversion. It's recreating the EXPERIENCE of reading in another language. The reader should feel what the original reader felt.

## Voice Preservation

The author's voice must survive translation:
- **Sentence rhythm:** If the author writes short, punchy sentences — keep them short in translation
- **Register:** Formal vs. informal must match (a sarcastic narrator stays sarcastic)
- **Vocabulary level:** Simple prose stays simple; literary prose stays literary
- **Dialog:** Character voices must remain distinct in translation

## Glossary-First Approach

Before translating a single chapter, build a glossary:

| Category | Decision | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Character names | Keep or adapt? | "John" stays "John" in German |
| Place names | Keep, translate, or adapt? | "Blackwood Forest" → "Schwarzwald" or keep? |
| Invented terms | Translate the meaning or keep? | "Shadowweave" → translate root words |
| Titles/ranks | Translate? | "Lord" → "Lord" or "Herr" or culture equivalent? |
| Idioms | Adapt to target culture | "It's raining cats and dogs" ≠ literal translation |

## Chapter-by-Chapter Workflow

1. **Read the entire chapter** before translating any line
2. **Translate in voice** — hearing the author in the target language
3. **Adapt cultural references** — find equivalents, don't explain
4. **Preserve wordplay** where possible — or create equivalent wordplay
5. **Check dialog** — does each character still sound distinct?
6. **Read aloud** — does the rhythm feel natural?

## Common Pitfalls

### False Friends
Words that look similar but mean different things:
- EN "actual" ≠ DE "aktuell" (current)
- EN "gift" ≠ DE "Gift" (poison)

### Structural Differences
- German: long compound words, verb-at-end in subclauses
- French: formal/informal "you" (tu/vous)
- Japanese: levels of politeness affecting word choice

### Humor
- Puns rarely translate — create new ones in the target language
- Cultural humor may need complete replacement
- Sarcasm registers differently across cultures

### Gendered Language
- Some languages have grammatical gender (German, French, Spanish)
- Non-binary pronouns: research target language solutions
- Character gender may affect every sentence structure

## Quality Checks

After completing all chapters:
- [ ] Glossary terms used consistently throughout
- [ ] Character voices remain distinct
- [ ] No untranslated passages accidentally left
- [ ] Cultural references adapted or explained
- [ ] Formatting preserved (italics, breaks, etc.)
- [ ] Read-aloud test: does it flow naturally?

## Tools

- **Pandoc:** Same export pipeline works for translated text
- **Glossary file:** Single source of truth for term consistency
- **Side-by-side review:** Compare original and translation chapter by chapter
