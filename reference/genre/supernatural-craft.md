# Supernatural Fiction Craft Reference

## Introduction: Building Believable Impossible Worlds

Supernatural fiction asks readers to accept premises that defy reality: vampires walk among us, the dead linger, demons bargain with humans, werewolves transform under the moon. The craft challenge is not in inventing these creatures—it's in building internally consistent mythologies that readers can inhabit without constant disbelief.

The best supernatural fiction treats its impossible elements as real within the world of the story. The writer doesn't wink at the reader; they commit fully to the logic of the fantastic. Consistency, psychological depth, and clear rules create believability for the unbelievable.

This document covers the major supernatural subgenres and the craft principles that make them work.

## 1. Vampire Fiction: Evolution and Approach

### Classical vs. Modern Vampires

**Classical Vampires (Stoker tradition):**
- Predatory, inhuman, monstrous
- Driven by hunger and supernatural compulsion
- Bound by specific rules: sunlight kills, holy symbols repel, crosses destroy
- Vampirism as curse or plague
- The vampire as ultimate Other—beautiful and terrible, seductive and evil

**Modern Vampires (Rice onwards):**
- Often sympathetic, even protagonists
- Struggle with morality, conscience, and the weight of immortality
- Rules are negotiable or invented
- Vampirism as gift, burden, or chosen path
- Exploration of vampire society, politics, philosophy

Both approaches are valid. The choice depends on what your story explores.

### The Rules Problem: Tradition vs. Subversion

Vampire fiction inherits rules from folklore and Stoker: sunlight burns, garlic repels, mirrors show no reflection, crosses cause pain, running water blocks them, etc.

**Questions to answer deliberately:**
- Which rules will your vampires follow?
- Which will you subvert? (And why?)
- Are the rules *magical* in nature or *biological*?
- Can the rules be broken by sufficiently powerful/old vampires?

Anne Rice's solution: Her vampires break most traditional rules. They're not bothered by crosses or garlic; they can see reflections; sunlight kills them not through magic but through biological vulnerability to UV exposure. Once Rice established her system, she maintained consistency within it.

**Critical principle:** Consistency matters more than tradition. If you choose to keep the garlic rule, maintain it. If you discard it, don't randomly invoke it later. Nothing breaks immersion faster than inconsistent magic system.

**Subversion strategy:** You can subvert a rule if the subversion serves the story. A vampire tormented by a religious faith they can no longer practice (because the holy symbols don't work as they were taught) has a different psychological burden than a vampire unaffected by such things. The subversion creates meaning.

### Vampire as Metaphor

Vampires are metaphorically rich. They can represent:

- **Addiction**: The vampire's hunger mirrors addiction—a compulsive need that overrides conscious choice, the constant negotiation with temptation, the possibility of abstinence.
- **Parasitism**: Living off others' energy or resources; feeding on relationships; the vampire as user.
- **Immortality and Boredom**: Eternal life stripping meaning from experience. How does one stay engaged with a world when you'll watch it repeat countless times?
- **Aristocracy and Class**: The vampire as ruling class, preying on the working class, existing in hidden hierarchies.
- **Sexual Danger**: The vampire's bite as seduction and assault, intimacy as penetration and violation, desire as dangerous.
- **Eternal Outsider**: The vampire as forever alone, unable to truly connect with mortals, forever hiding.

Consider which metaphorical resonance your vampire should carry. If your vampire is primarily an exploration of addiction, the rules and character psychology should reflect that. If the vampire explores aristocracy and power, the mythology should include social hierarchy.

### Vampire Society and Politics

Modern vampire fiction often includes vampire societies—governance structures, hierarchies, laws. This creates rich possibilities for conflict and worldbuilding.

**Questions to explore:**
- Is there a vampire government? A council? A prince?
- What are vampire laws, and what are the punishments for breaking them?
- How do young vampires learn? Is there mentorship or training?
- How do different vampire "bloodlines" or "nests" relate to each other?
- Are vampires public knowledge or hidden?

Anne Rice's later work (*The Prince Lestat* onwards) evolved to include more developed vampire society, councils, and modern integration (vampire podcasts, global communication networks). This reflects how vampire fiction has grown to explore institutional questions, not just individual psychology.

### Loneliness and Connection

A core tension in vampire fiction: immortal creatures dependent on mortals for food, yet unable to truly bond with them. The vampire is forever separated from human experience—aging, natural death, the rhythms of generational change.

**Psychological depth:** Explore what it means to be *functionally* immortal. Does the vampire become detached and nihilistic? Do they form attachments despite knowing the person will die? Do they embrace killing to feel alive? Do they seek other vampires for connection, even when it's dangerous?

The finest vampire fiction often hinges on this loneliness—the vampire's simultaneous predatory nature and desperate need for connection.

## 2. Werewolf Fiction: Transformation and Duality

### Transformation as Metaphor

Werewolf transformation is metaphorically richer than it initially appears. It can represent:

- **Loss of Control**: The moment where civilization, consciousness, and will are stripped away, revealing the primal self beneath. Useful for exploring anxieties about anger, violence, or sexuality.
- **Duality of Self**: The human and animal as two selves in conflict. Jekyll-and-Hyde themes. The beast within.
- **Adolescence**: Transformation often mirrors puberty—the body changing, new urges emerging, losing control of one's own form.
- **Suppressed Identity**: Particularly in LGBTQ+ fiction, the werewolf can represent a hidden self demanding to be expressed.
- **Trauma**: Physical transformation can metaphorically express psychological trauma—the self becoming strange, hostile, unrecognizable.

The transformation scene is central to werewolf fiction. It's both physical spectacle and psychological event. The best werewolf fiction treats transformation as genuinely difficult—not a superhero power-up but a loss and violation of the self.

### The "Rules" of Lycanthropy

Like vampire fiction, werewolf fiction must establish clear rules:

- **Trigger**: Full moon only? At will? During emotional stress? During specific lunar phases? Can the werewolf choose whether to transform?
- **Duration**: Transformation lasts for minutes? Hours? The entire night?
- **Consciousness**: Is the werewolf-self aware? Can it think? Does it remember what happens in wolf form?
- **Spread**: Can werewolfism be transmitted through bites? Is it hereditary? Can it be caught like a disease?
- **Weaknesses**: What can hurt a werewolf in either form? Silver? Wolfsbane? Fire? Can they be killed?
- **Agency**: Can the person resist transformation? Can they control the beast's actions?

Kelley Armstrong (*Bitten*) uses at-will transformation, treating it as a power. Laurell K. Hamilton's (*Anita Blake*) series uses lunar compulsion combined with some agent control. The differences create different story possibilities.

**Consistency check:** If transformation is involuntary and removes conscious control, the werewolf character's moral culpability changes. They can't be blamed for harm done in wolf form. Conversely, if transformation is chosen, they bear responsibility.

### Pack Dynamics

Werewolf fiction frequently includes pack hierarchy—alpha, beta, omega rankings. This creates instant social structure and conflict potential.

**Worldbuilding considerations:**
- Is the pack a family unit or a predatory hierarchy?
- How rigid is the hierarchy? Can members challenge for status?
- How do pack members relate to non-pack werewolves?
- What loyalty do they owe each other?
- Can a werewolf survive outside the pack?

Pack dynamics are useful for exploring questions of belonging, loyalty, power, and community. But avoid making pack hierarchy purely about domination and submission—real packs (human or wolf) have complex social structures.

### The Curse vs. The Gift Debate

Werewolfism can be presented as either:

- **A Curse**: Something the character didn't choose, can't escape, that isolates them and makes them dangerous. The werewolf as victim of circumstance.
- **A Gift**: Strength, speed, senses, and power. Freedom from human constraint. The werewolf as elevated beyond humanity.

The best werewolf fiction often presents both dimensions—the curse of loss of control and the gift of power, each character weighing them differently.

### The Beast Within

Werewolf fiction frequently explores the "beast within"—the idea that humans contain animal appetites and instincts that civilization suppresses. Transformation makes literal what is metaphorically true: we contain multiple selves.

This is useful for exploring questions about morality, responsibility, and human nature. To what degree is the werewolf responsible for harm done in animal form? What does the existence of the beast say about the human?

## 3. Ghost Stories: Haunting and Interiority

### The Ghost as Unfinished Business

The most effective ghost stories typically involve a ghost with a specific, powerful motivation:

- **Unresolved murder**: The ghost seeks justice or revenge
- **Unjust death**: The ghost protests against the wrong done to them
- **Lost love**: The ghost cannot let go of an attachment
- **Betrayal**: The ghost torments those who wronged them
- **Unfinished work**: The ghost must complete something left undone

The best ghost stories invest the ghost with *interiority*—we understand their emotional and psychological state. They're not simply a scary force; they're a human consciousness in extremis.

**Example:** In *The Haunting of Hill House* by Shirley Jackson, the house itself may be haunted, but the psychological haunting—the ways the house exploits Eleanor's loneliness and psychological vulnerability—is the real horror.

### The Haunted House as Psyche

A useful way to think about haunted house narratives: the house is an externalization of the protagonist's mind. The rooms are compartments of consciousness, the hauntings are psychological disturbances, and exorcising the ghost is processing internal trauma.

This doesn't mean ghosts are "just in their heads"—it means the ghost story *structure* mirrors psychological experience. A haunting that becomes more intense the more the protagonist ignores it parallels how repressed trauma intensifies.

### Ghost Rules and Limitations

Ghosts need clear parameters:

- **Manifestation**: Can the ghost interact with the physical world? Move objects? Appear visually?
- **Location**: Is the ghost tied to a specific place? Can they leave?
- **Awareness**: Are ghosts aware of time passing? Can they perceive the living?
- **Communication**: Can they speak? Are they limited to signs?
- **Binding**: What keeps a ghost bound to the world? How can they be released?
- **Power**: Do ghosts grow stronger or weaker over time? Can they hurt the living?

Establishing these rules matters because they determine what the ghost can and cannot do, which shapes plot possibilities and creates narrative tension.

**Consistency matters:** If a ghost can only manifest through cold spots in Chapter 3, they can't suddenly throw furniture in Chapter 10 without explanation.

### Atmospheric Ghost Stories vs. Action-Driven Ghost Stories

**Atmospheric:** Focus on mood, psychological unease, and the creeping sense of wrongness. The ghost may never be directly encountered. Dread accumulates through environment and ambiguity. Examples: *The Turn of the Screw* by Henry James, *The Haunting of Hill House*.

**Action-driven:** The ghost actively pursues, reveals, demands, or torments. There's a clear conflict between ghost and living characters. Examples: *Poltergeist*, *The Sixth Sense*, supernatural revenge narratives.

Both approaches are valid. Atmospheric ghost stories demand more subtlety in rendering dread. Action-driven stories need clear motivation and stakes.

### The Interpretive Question

The classic ghost story question: Is the haunting real or psychological? The best ghost stories leave this genuinely ambiguous. The reader (and perhaps the characters) cannot be certain whether supernatural forces are at work or whether the protagonist is descending into madness.

*The Turn of the Screw* famously sustains this ambiguity across its entire length. The reader must decide whether the governess is protecting children from real ghosts or torturing them with her own delusions.

**Craft principle:** Ambiguity serves atmospheric horror better than clear confirmation. The fear of not knowing, of the boundary between real and unreal dissolving, is more unsettling than seeing the ghost clearly.

## 4. Demon and Angel Fiction: Theology and Free Will

### Theological Lore: Use or Subvert

Demon and angel fiction can:

- **Use traditional theology**: Build on Christian, Islamic, Jewish, Hindu, or Buddhist cosmologies, respecting their internal logic.
- **Create new theology**: Invent your own hierarchies, rules, and metaphysical structures.
- **Subvert expectations**: Invert traditional roles (demons with mercy, angels with cruelty), challenge assumptions about good and evil.

Each approach demands research. If you use existing theological frameworks, your readers who know those frameworks will notice errors or inconsistencies. If you invent new ones, consistency within your system is all that matters.

**Example of use:** In Gaiman and Pratchett's *Good Omens*, the angel Aziraphale and demon Crowley operate within Christian cosmology but with a subversive twist: they've become friends across the Heaven/Hell divide, complicating traditional binary morality.

**Example of invention:** In Naomi Novik's *Uprooted*, the evil Wood and the magic system operate outside any traditional theology but maintain strict internal consistency.

### Hierarchy and Politics

Supernatural hierarchies create instant conflict and structure. If heaven and hell have institutions, they have politics.

**Questions to explore:**
- Is there a clear hierarchy? Kings, presidents, councils, autonomous beings?
- Are angels or demons bound to follow orders? Can they rebel?
- What are the conflicts between different factions?
- What are the stakes in the supernatural war?
- Can lower-order beings negotiate with higher-order ones?

John Grisham's *Sycamore Row* and other contemporary fantasy novels often include complex supernatural bureaucracies, where the real conflict emerges from competing interests rather than simple good vs. evil.

### Free Will vs. Predestination

A central theological question: If a supernatural being is created for a purpose, do they have free will? If heaven has a plan, can individual actors choose otherwise?

This philosophical tension creates psychological depth. An angel bound to serve heaven but developing individual conscience. A demon ordered to corrupt but finding unexpected compassion. The character's struggle against their created nature is itself the drama.

### Moral Absolutism vs. Moral Complexity

Supernatural fiction can present morality as:

- **Absolute**: Good is good, evil is evil, unambiguously. The story explores the cost of choosing a side.
- **Complex**: Both heaven and hell are flawed. Both contain cruelty and grace. Characters must navigate moral gray areas.

The best contemporary supernatural fiction tends toward complexity. Readers are sophisticated enough to recognize that genuine institutions (even divine ones) contain corruption, self-interest, and moral compromise.

## 5. Mythology Consistency: Building Believable Lore

### Choosing Your Mythological Foundation

You can:

- **Use existing mythology**: Draw on Norse, Greek, Mesopotamian, Hindu, Indigenous, or other traditions.
- **Create new mythology**: Invent your own cosmology from scratch.
- **Blend traditions**: Carefully combine elements from different mythologies in deliberate ways.

Each has implications:

- **Existing mythology** requires research but brings cultural resonance. Readers familiar with the source material will notice errors.
- **New mythology** demands total internal consistency but risks feeling arbitrary without grounding.
- **Blended mythology** risks confusion if the blending lacks clear logic.

**Principle:** Whatever you choose, commit to its logic. Don't randomly mix vampire rules from different sources, angel hierarchies from different religions, or werewolf transformations from conflicting traditions—unless that mixing is itself meaningful (as when discovering different mythologies exist in your world).

### The Consistency Test

Once you've established your supernatural rules, test them:

- If vampires are immune to crosses, why would sunlight kill them? (Religious symbolism vs. biological vulnerability).
- If werewolves can transform at will, why would they ever stay human during danger?
- If demons can be summoned and bound, why wouldn't every sorcerer do this constantly?
- If ghosts can move objects, why can't they directly communicate?

Inconsistencies break immersion. Readers notice and become skeptical. Either explain the apparent contradiction or adjust your rules.

### Documentation: Creating a Mythology Bible

For complex supernatural worldbuilding, consider creating a "mythology bible" or lore document for yourself:

- What are the origins of supernatural creatures?
- What are their powers and limitations?
- How do they relate to each other?
- What secret history exists?
- What are the rules governing magic or supernatural forces?
- What do humans know vs. what is hidden?

This document doesn't appear in your story, but it prevents inconsistencies. Characters can be consistent because *you* are consistent.

### Revealing Lore Without Info-Dumping

A temptation in supernatural fiction: dump worldbuilding exposition on readers through dialogue or narration. This is deadly.

**Better strategies:**
- Reveal lore gradually through characters *acting* based on the rules (demonstrating rather than explaining)
- Use discovery scenes where characters learn rules alongside readers
- Plant hints earlier that pay off later when full context emerges
- Let some lore remain mysterious; readers don't need to know everything
- Use conflict that emerges from characters *disagreeing* about lore or its interpretation

**Example:** In *The Dresden Files*, readers gradually learn about the magical world through Harry Dresden's discoveries and interactions. We don't get a manual of magical rules; we experience them as Harry does.

## 6. Lore-Building: Depth and Mystery

### Ancient Origins and Hidden Wars

The most interesting supernatural lore includes history: When did vampires arise? Who created werewolves? How long have demons been meddling in human affairs?

This history doesn't need to be fully revealed. In fact, *partial* revelation is often more interesting. Creatures might disagree about their own origins. Ancient history might be mythologized, making its truth unknowable. Hidden wars might have shaped the world in ways characters don't fully understand.

**Technique:** Create layers of knowledge:
- What humans generally believe (often wrong)
- What supernatural creatures know (contested and incomplete)
- What the reader gradually discovers (approaching truth)
- What remains hidden (keeping mystery alive)

### Secret Societies and Hidden Powers

Hidden organizations—councils, covens, cabals—allow supernatural forces to operate within the human world undetected. This creates inherent conflict: maintaining secrecy vs. pursuing goals.

**Worldbuilding questions:**
- Who polices the supernatural world?
- What are the consequences for revealing the supernatural?
- Are there factions within secret societies?
- How much do humans know or suspect?

### Mythology as Living Conflict

The most engaging lore involves *disagreement*. Different creatures interpret their shared history differently. Factions have competing versions of origin stories. Prophecies are contested.

This is more interesting than static mythology because it creates character motivation. Two creatures might battle partly because they disagree fundamentally about what the lore means.

## 7. Transformation as Theme: Identity and Humanity

### Physical Change as Psychological Mirror

Transformation scenes should reflect not just physical change but psychological states. A peaceful transformation suggests acceptance; a violent one suggests struggle. Quick transformation suggests inevitability; slow transformation suggests resistance.

Use the *mechanics* of transformation to communicate emotional content.

### Losing and Finding Humanity

A key question in transformation fiction: What does it mean to become something other? Does the human self persist? Does consciousness change?

Werewolf fiction often explores this: Does the wolf-self have different morality? Different desires? Is the person in wolf form "really them"?

Vampire fiction explores it too: Do vampires retain humanity after transformation, or does something essential change? Can they still love? Feel remorse?

This psychological question is often more interesting than the physical mechanics.

### Identity Crisis Through Metamorphosis

Transformation can create genuine identity crisis. A character must reconcile human self with animal self, mortal self with undead self, human consciousness with inhuman appetites.

The best transformation narratives treat this seriously. The character doesn't simply gain a new ability; they must integrate a fundamental change into their sense of self.

## 8. Power Dynamics: Immortal and Mortal Relationships

### The Imbalance Problem

An immortal and a mortal in relationship face inherent imbalance:

- **Lifespan**: The mortal will age and die; the immortal watches them deteriorate.
- **Power**: The immortal typically has superhuman abilities; the mortal is fragile.
- **Knowledge**: The immortal has centuries of experience; the mortal is learning.
- **Perspective**: What matters urgently to the mortal seems trivial to the immortal who's seen it all before.

These imbalances are *dramatically interesting* because they're genuinely difficult to reconcile.

### Avoiding Problematic Power Dynamics

A critical consideration: How much does power imbalance enable abuse?

If one character can physically overpower, mentally dominate, or control the other, the relationship risks becoming abusive regardless of intentions. Supernatural power imbalances can romanticize dynamic that would be recognized as abusive in realistic fiction.

**Craft consideration:** Either address the power imbalance explicitly (characters acknowledging and negotiating it) or adjust the premise so imbalance is less extreme.

**Example of addressing it well:** In some paranormal romance, the supernatural character deliberately *limits* their power in the relationship, choosing vulnerability as a sign of trust.

### Immortal Detachment

How does an immortal being relate emotionally to mortals? Some possibilities:

- **Protective detachment**: "I will care for you, but I know you'll die. How do I protect myself from that grief?"
- **Desperate attachment**: "You're temporary. I'll love you fiercely anyway, knowing this is borrowed time."
- **Philosophical acceptance**: "All creatures die. Your mortality doesn't make you less worthy of love."
- **Nihilistic distance**: "You'll die like everyone. Why bother?"

These different approaches create different emotional textures and character depths.

## 9. Immortality as Narrative Challenge: Boredom and Meaning

### The Problem of Eternal Boredom

An immortal creature might experience:

- **Repetition**: Seeing the same events, patterns, and human dramas repeat across centuries. Nothing feels new or surprising.
- **Loss of stakes**: If you're immortal, death doesn't threaten you. Do mortal struggles matter?
- **Detachment**: As centuries pass, do individual people become less meaningful? Do you stop caring?

This is psychologically realistic. A being who's lived 500 years has *experienced* so much that new experience becomes difficult to process.

### Ancient Perspective and Distance

How does an immortal perceive mortality? They might:

- **Pity mortals**: From a distance, human lives seem impossibly brief, their struggles quaint.
- **Envy mortals**: The mortality gives meaning; the finitude makes each moment matter.
- **Observe mortals**: Study them like anthropologists, fascinated by their novelty.
- **Despise mortals**: See them as ephemeral insects, unworthy of real engagement.

Different perspectives create different character archetypes.

### Staying Engaged: What Keeps Immortals Alive

The best immortal character portrayals include explanation for how they've sustained meaning:

- **Purpose**: A mission or goal that extends beyond individual lifetimes (revenge, prophecy fulfillment, world change).
- **Relationships**: Connection to specific people or communities that centers their meaning.
- **Scholarship**: The pursuit of knowledge or mastery that's endless.
- **Art or Creation**: Making things becomes the meaning.
- **Curiosity**: Genuine interest in how the world unfolds.

Without such anchoring, immortality becomes a burden that would rationally lead to despair or nihilism.

## 10. Classical vs. Modern Interpretation: Honoring and Subverting Tradition

### When to Honor Tradition

Readers come to vampire, werewolf, and ghost fiction with expectations shaped by tradition. Honoring those expectations can ground your work in a literary tradition and create pleasurable recognition.

**Traditional vampire tropes:**
- Beautiful but dangerous
- Aristocratic, cultured, separated from human morality
- Driven by sexual predation
- Cursed or damned
- Bound by strict rules

**Traditional werewolf tropes:**
- Cursed or monstrous
- Struggling against their nature
- Isolated by their condition
- Powerful but uncontrolled

**Traditional ghost tropes:**
- Unquiet dead
- Seeking justice or communication
- Tied to a location or person
- Mysterious and otherworldly

When you honor these traditions, readers immediately *know* what kind of story they're entering.

### When to Subvert

Subversion works when the inversion itself serves the story:

- A vampire who doesn't desire blood; a vampire who wants to become human.
- A werewolf who embraces transformation; a werewolf who finds peace in their nature.
- A ghost who is happy and not seeking anything; a ghost who is more present than the living.

Subversion should have *meaning*. It's not subversion just to be different; it's exploring what becomes possible when you flip the traditional assumption.

### Urban Fantasy Hybrids

Contemporary supernatural fiction often blends genres: vampire detectives, werewolf teachers, ghost therapists. These roles create instant tension with traditional character expectations.

The craft challenge: Make the character's supernatural nature and their mundane role interact meaningfully. A vampire detective investigating murders explores themes of predation, hunger, and moral compromise in new ways. A ghost therapist explores themes of listening, witnessing, and healing.

## 11. Key Authors and Reference Points

### Anne Rice: Vampire Psychology and Immortal Society

Rice transformed vampire fiction from monsters to complex beings with elaborate inner lives. Her vampires are seductive, articulate, and philosophically engaged with their immortality.

**Key contribution:** Moving vampire fiction from external action (hunting, fighting) to internal psychology (desire, meaning, isolation).

### Neil Gaiman: Mythological Synthesis and Wonder

Gaiman blends mythology, folklore, and contemporary settings, creating worlds where ancient and modern coexist. His supernatural creatures feel real within his mythological frameworks.

**Key contribution:** Making lore feel alive and contested rather than static; grounding the fantastic in character and emotion.

### Charlaine Harris: Urban Supernatural and Community

The Sookie Stackhouse series situates supernatural creatures within contemporary communities, exploring how the supernatural would actually integrate (or fail to integrate) into modern life.

**Key contribution:** Treating supernatural revelation as a social and political issue; creating ensemble casts where multiple perspectives on the supernatural exist.

### V.E. Schwab: Power Systems and Dark Fantasy

Schwab's work features elaborate supernatural systems with clear rules and costs. Magic and supernatural power always exact a price.

**Key contribution:** Creating supernatural systems where power is constrained, costly, and psychologically consequential.

### Leigh Bardugo: Dark Fairy Tale and Morally Complex Characters

Bardugo's work reimagines fairy tale and fantasy archetypes with morally complex characters whose supernatural natures don't make them good or evil.

**Key contribution:** Creating sympathetic characters within dark supernatural frameworks; treating the "villain's" perspective with genuine depth.

## Practical Guidance for Writing Supernatural Fiction

### Start with Rules, Not Cool Powers

Before deciding how your creature *looks* or what cool abilities they have, establish the *rules* that govern them. The rules create constraints, and constraints create dramatic possibility.

### Make the Supernatural Serve Themes

Don't include supernatural elements just because they're cool. Ensure your creature type explores something thematically meaningful. A vampire story about addiction. A werewolf story about repressed identity. A ghost story about unresolved trauma.

### Test for Consistency

Regularly ask: Is this consistent with what I've established? If not, either explain the contradiction or adjust the rules.

### Ground the Fantastic in the Real

The more fantastic your supernatural elements, the more grounded your characters, relationships, and settings should be. Supernatural fiction works through contrast between the impossible and the intimate.

### Explore the Psychological Consequences

Immortality, predatory hunger, transformation, the ability to perceive the dead—these aren't just plot devices. They're profoundly psychologically consequential. Explore how they would actually affect a human consciousness.
