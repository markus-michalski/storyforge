# Science Fiction Craft Reference

## Hard Sci-Fi vs. Soft Sci-Fi: Understanding the Spectrum

Science fiction operates along a spectrum from hard (scientifically rigorous) to soft (socially/philosophically focused). Understanding where your story sits determines how you'll approach worldbuilding, research, and the balance between concept and character.

### Hard Science Fiction

Hard sci-fi prioritizes scientific accuracy and plausibility. The "hard" refers to the hard sciences: physics, chemistry, biology, astronomy.

**Characteristics**:
- Rigorous extrapolation from actual science.
- Technical accuracy matters. Readers often include scientists, engineers, and subject matter experts.
- Technology and scientific problems drive the plot.
- Solutions are rooted in science and problem-solving.
- World-building is bottom-up: start with plausible physics, resource constraints, and technological limitations, then build society from those constraints.

**Examples**:
- **Isaac Asimov**: Robot stories and Foundation series. Explored how technology functions and how robots and humans might coexist. Established the Three Laws of Robotics as thought experiment.
- **Arthur C. Clarke**: 2001: A Space Odyssey, Rendezvous with Rama. Detailed extrapolation of space travel physics, orbital mechanics, and alien contact scenarios.
- **Andy Weir**: The Martian, Project Hail Mary. Problem-solving narratives where the protagonist must use physics, chemistry, and engineering to survive.

**Key craft principle**: In hard sci-fi, the problem statement and the solution matter as much as character development. Readers are drawn to the intellectual challenge. However, this doesn't mean hard sci-fi lacks character—Weir's The Martian works because Mark Watney is compelling and his personality shines through his technical problem-solving.

### Soft Science Fiction

Soft sci-fi emphasizes the social, psychological, political, or anthropological consequences of technology. The "soft" refers to the soft sciences: sociology, psychology, political science, anthropology.

**Characteristics**:
- Scientific plausibility is secondary to thematic exploration.
- The technology or speculative premise may be hand-waved; the "how" matters less than the "what if."
- Explores how technology affects human society, identity, relationships, and values.
- World-building is top-down: start with the thematic question and society's response, then invent technology to support that world.
- Character development and human experience are central.

**Examples**:
- **Ursula K. Le Guin**: The Left Hand of Darkness explores gender and society on an alien world. The planet's biology creates a gender-fluid society; the scientific accuracy of that biology is less important than what it reveals about human assumptions around gender.
- **Ray Bradbury**: The Martian Chronicles. Stories about colonization and human nature, not hard science. Bradbury famously said he wrote about people, not technology.
- **Star Wars, Star Trek**: These are soft sci-fi space operas. The technology (lightsabers, transporters) isn't scientifically plausible, but the social and political implications are explored.
- **Octavia Butler**: Parable of the Sower, Dawn. Explores power, adaptation, and human nature in speculative futures. Technology is present but not the focus.

**Key craft principle**: In soft sci-fi, the speculative premise is the vehicle for exploring human questions. The technology or alien world is the mechanism; the story is about identity, society, survival, or meaning.

### The Spectrum

Hard and soft aren't binaries; they're endpoints on a spectrum. Most published sci-fi blends elements of both:

- **Hard-leaning**: Technical accuracy matters, but character and theme are present.
- **Center**: Equal weight on technology/problem-solving and character/society.
- **Soft-leaning**: Speculative worldbuilding provides backdrop for character-driven narrative, but internal consistency matters.

Know where your story sits and commit to it. Hard sci-fi readers will notice scientific errors; soft sci-fi readers care more about emotional truth and theme.

## The "What If" Question: The Foundation of Sci-Fi

Every science fiction story begins with a single speculative premise—a "what if" question:

- What if faster-than-light travel was possible?
- What if artificial intelligence became sentient?
- What if humans could live forever?
- What if society organized around a resource scarcity?
- What if genders were fluid?
- What if we could travel through time?

This speculative premise is *the* idea. It's the one big lie—the one way in which your world differs from the real world. Everything else flows from that premise.

### The Rule of Consistent Extrapolation

Once you establish your speculative premise, extrapolate rigorously. Work out the consequences—not just the immediate, obvious ones, but the ripple effects across technology, society, economics, and human behavior.

**Example**: If faster-than-light travel exists:
- What does that mean for planetary governance? (Planets are now reachable; interplanetary empires become possible.)
- What does that mean for resource distribution? (No planetary scarcity if you can mine asteroids or travel to habitable worlds.)
- What does that mean for society? (Migration becomes viable; cultural mixing increases.)
- What does that mean for conflict? (Territory and resources shift from planetary to interplanetary.)

Your story doesn't need to explore *all* consequences, but the ones you ignore shouldn't create plot holes. If FTL travel exists but you write a story about resource scarcity on a single planet, explain why FTL isn't solving that problem.

### One Big Lie, Everything Else True

Keep the number of speculative premises minimal. One central "lie" (deviation from reality) is ideal. Multiple speculative premises become exponentially harder to manage.

A story with both FTL travel AND time travel AND sentient AI needs to work out how all three interact. Usually, this creates confusion. Stick to one or two core speculative elements and make everything else as realistic as possible.

The more elements of reality you preserve, the more plausible the speculative element feels. A story set in recognizable 2050s Earth with one speculative premise (AI consciousness) feels more believable than a story with five different future technologies layered on top of each other.

## Technology as Metaphor: The Human Question

The best science fiction uses technology as a vehicle to explore deeply human questions. The technology should illuminate, not obscure, the human element.

### Common Technology-as-Metaphor Pairings

**Artificial Intelligence = Consciousness and Free Will**
- What makes consciousness "real"?
- Can intelligence without biological substrate have personhood?
- Who decides the rights of sentient beings?
- Examples: Asimov's robots, Philip K. Dick's androids, Ex Machina.

**Time Travel = Regret and Fate**
- Can the past be changed, or is time fixed?
- What would you change if you could?
- Do choices matter if the future is predetermined?
- Examples: The Terminator, 12 Monkeys, All You Need Is Kill (Edge of Tomorrow).

**Space Exploration = Isolation and Discovery**
- What does humanity lose when separated from Earth?
- How does isolation reshape human identity and society?
- What does encounter with the truly alien reveal about ourselves?
- Examples: Solaris, The Expanse, Rendezvous with Rama.

**Genetic Engineering = Identity and Humanity**
- What makes someone "human"?
- Can designer humans have equal rights?
- Where's the line between enhancement and violation?
- Examples: Brave New World, Gataca, GATTACA.

**Cloning = Uniqueness and Soul**
- Is a clone a copy or a new person?
- Can duplicates have separate identities?
- What's lost if perfect replication is possible?
- Examples: Orphan Black, The Island, Never Let Me Go.

**Virtual Reality = Reality and Identity**
- What's "real" if perception is what matters?
- Can virtual identity be as valid as biological identity?
- How does extended time in simulation change humanity?
- Examples: The Matrix, Ready Player One, Neuromancer.

### Integrating Metaphor into Plot

The technology-as-metaphor works best when it's *embedded in the plot*, not just thematic wallpaper:

- A character must make a choice about the technology that forces them to confront the underlying question.
- The technology's limitations or capabilities directly create plot complications.
- The resolution (if any) of the technological question mirrors the character's internal resolution.

In Asimov's Robot series, a robot's adherence to the Three Laws creates plot complications because they're designed to explore what consciousness and free will mean. The robot's dilemma is the reader's dilemma.

## World-Building Without Info-Dumps: Show, Don't Tell

The biggest sci-fi mistake is stopping the story to explain how things work. Readers don't need to understand the physics; they need to understand the *consequences*.

### The "Used Future" Aesthetic

The most convincing sci-fi makes technology feel ordinary, not wondrous. Characters don't explain things they take for granted.

In Star Wars, we don't get a lecture on how lightsabers work. Obi-Wan hands Luke a lightsaber, Luke activates it (it hums, it's heavy, it's clearly dangerous), and they move on. We understand it's a tool, important and valuable, but not in need of explanation.

The "used future" means:
- Technology is worn, scratched, repaired, modified.
- Characters interact with it casually, like we interact with smartphones (without wondering how cellular networks function).
- Exposition comes through action and dialogue, not lengthy description.

### Showing Technology Through Character Interaction

When you need to convey how technology works, show a character *using* it:

Instead of: "The warp drive works by folding spacetime, which allows ships to travel faster than light without violating relativistic physics..."

Do this:
```
Captain Chen entered the coordinates into the nav console. The ship hummed, and 
the stars outside began to stretch. Her stomach lurched as the drive engaged—
that familiar sense of reality shifting. In thirty seconds, they'd be light-years away.
```

The reader understands: the drive is active, it takes a few seconds, it causes physical sensation, and it moves the ship vast distances. All without an explanation.

### Burying Exposition in Dialogue

When characters discuss technology, they discuss the *consequence*, not the mechanics:

Instead of: "Quantum entanglement allows instant communication across any distance..."

Do this:
```
"Have you received a message from the colony yet?"
"Just came through via quantum link. They're stable, but supplies are low."
```

The reader learns: quantum links exist, they transmit messages, they work across distance, and they're reliable. They don't learn the physics, and they don't need to.

### Info-Dumps as Last Resort

Sometimes, you must explain something complex. In that case:
- Embed it in dialogue between characters (not a narrator explaining to the reader).
- Keep it brief.
- Follow immediately with action or consequence.
- Make it necessary to the plot.

Andy Weir's The Martian includes technical explanations, but they're filtered through Mark Watney's character voice and always tied to solving an immediate problem. We understand the rocket equation not because we need to, but because Mark is calculating fuel requirements to survive.

### The White Room Syndrome

Avoid "white room" syndrome: characters in an empty space discussing the world. They're in a spaceship/colony/city—show it. The environment provides exposition through sensory detail:

Instead of: "The colony had been established fifteen years ago and was now home to two thousand people..."

Do this:
```
Sarah climbed the stairs toward hab-sector three, passing second-generation colonists—
kids who'd never breathed Earth air. The walls were etched with fifteen years of wear, 
sealed cracks and fresh patches marking different construction phases.
```

The reader learns: the colony is old, it's stable, it's large, and it's showing age. All through the character's movement through the environment.

## Plausibility Rules: Internal Consistency Over Real-World Accuracy

Science fiction doesn't need to match real-world science; it needs internal consistency. If faster-than-light travel exists in your world, work out the implications and stick to them.

### The Prime Rule: Establish and Maintain

1. Establish the rule: "In this world, FTL travel takes three weeks minimum."
2. Maintain the rule: Don't suddenly have a character FTL across the galaxy in three days to serve plot convenience.
3. Work with limitations: Build plot tension around the limitation.

If you establish that FTL takes three weeks, a scene where a character must reach a destination in two weeks creates genuine tension. You've set up your own constraints and now must work within them.

### Avoid Cherry-Picking Consequences

The biggest plausibility error is cherry-picking which consequences of your technology to acknowledge:

- If cloning is possible, work out the legal/ethical implications. Don't have cloning exist but never address whether clones have rights.
- If telepathy is possible, work out privacy implications. Don't have telepaths reading minds without consequences.
- If time travel exists, address paradoxes. Ignoring them for plot convenience breaks immersion.

You don't need to solve every problem, but don't pretend it doesn't exist.

### Internal Logic Over External Logic

A rule that makes sense within your world is better than a rule that matches real-world science but feels arbitrary:

"Telepathy works across short distances (50 meters) because of neural field limitations" makes sense within the world.

"Telepathy works on Tuesdays" needs justification.

The rule doesn't need to match physics; it needs to be internally logical and consistently applied.

## Space Opera: Grand Scale and Political Intrigue

Space opera emphasizes scope, political complexity, and character within a vast cosmos.

**Characteristics**:
- Multiple planets, star systems, or civilizations.
- Political intrigue, wars, and empire-building.
- Epic scope: the story matters on a galactic scale.
- Character is central: individuals affect galactic events.
- Series-friendly: multiple books allow for deep worldbuilding.

**Examples**:
- **Dune**: Planetary feudalism, religion, ecology, and politics. A character's choices reshape civilizations.
- **The Expanse**: Interplanetary political tension, asteroid mining colonies, and the discovery of ancient alien technology.
- **Hyperion**: Multiple POV characters, time-spanning narrative, and a universe-spanning threat.

**Key craft principle**: Space opera works because readers care about individual characters navigating vast systems. The scale makes character choices feel weighty. A character's decision affects millions, so their internal conflicts matter more.

## Dystopian and Post-Apocalyptic: Society as Warning

These subgenres explore how society can fail or collapse. The speculative premise is usually social/political rather than technological.

### Dystopia (Fictional Society Gone Wrong)

**Characteristics**:
- Explores how a society becomes oppressive or broken.
- Often extrapolates from current trends (surveillance, authoritarianism, inequality) taken to extremes.
- The system is the antagonist.
- Usually set in a believable near-future.

**Examples**:
- **1984**: Totalitarianism and surveillance pushed to absolute control.
- **Handmaid's Tale**: Religious fundamentalism and gender-based oppression.
- **The Giver**: Conformity and the elimination of choice/emotion.

**Key craft principle**: The best dystopias feel *plausible*. Extrapolate from real trends. "This government is evil for no reason" isn't as effective as "this government arose from rational (but flawed) responses to crisis, and we've reached unintended consequences." Readers recognize themselves in the choices that created the dystopia.

### Post-Apocalyptic (Civilization Collapsed)

**Characteristics**:
- Society has broken down from some catastrophe (plague, war, climate, meteor).
- Explores survival and human nature without societal infrastructure.
- Often smaller scale than dystopia: focused on specific communities or characters.
- Tone varies: hopeful rebuilding, grim survival, or bitter loss.

**Examples**:
- **The Road**: Post-apocalyptic bleakness and the bonds between people.
- **Parable of the Sower**: Climate collapse and the creation of new social structures.
- **Station Eleven**: Pandemic collapse and the resilience of human culture and story.

**Key craft principle**: Post-apocalyptic works because it strips away civilization and asks what remains. What do humans value when survival is primary? What does community mean without institutions? Character and philosophy matter more than plot mechanics.

## Cyberpunk: High Tech, Low Life

Cyberpunk explores technology's dark side and corporate power in futures where the gap between rich and poor is extreme.

**Characteristics**:
- Advanced technology (AI, cybernetics, neural interface) controlled by corporations or governments.
- Street-level perspective: protagonists are usually outsiders, hackers, or low-status people using technology to survive or rebel.
- Themes: corporate control, identity in digital age, body modification, information as power.
- Aesthetic: neon, decay, digital/physical hybridity.

**Examples**:
- **Neuromancer**: Hackers in a future of corporate dominance and artificial intelligence.
- **Snow Crash**: Virtual reality, corporate city-states, and digital identity.
- **Altered Carbon**: Mind uploading, immortality for the rich, and inequality.

**Key craft principle**: Cyberpunk works because it explores power imbalances through technology. The hacker protagonist doesn't have wealth or authority; they have skills and knowledge that let them navigate systems. The tension is David-versus-Goliath: individual versus corporate/government power.

## Time Travel: Managing Paradox and Emotional Core

Time travel is complicated because of paradoxes. Establish your time travel rules early and stick to them.

### Time Travel Rules: Three Models

**Fixed Timeline (Bootstrap Paradox)**
- The past is unchangeable. Attempts to alter the past fail or were always part of history.
- Complexity: characters may create the very thing they're trying to prevent.
- Example: 12 Monkeys. The protagonist can't prevent the apocalypse; their actions *cause* it.

**Mutable Timeline (Grandfather Paradox)**
- The past can be changed, which alters the future.
- Complexity: changes to the past may create time paradoxes or split timelines.
- Example: The Terminator. Changing the past creates a new future.

**Multiverse (Branching Timelines)**
- Traveling to the past creates a branching timeline; you can't change your own past, only create an alternate future.
- Complexity: less paradox, more responsibility to alternate timelines.
- Example: Avengers: Endgame. Characters take from alternate timelines, creating branches.

Pick one model and stick with it. Mixing models confuses readers and breaks plausibility.

### Emotional Core Over Mechanics

The reason time travel works as narrative isn't the mechanics; it's the emotional core: *What would you change if you could?*

The best time travel stories answer this question:
- In 12 Monkeys, the protagonist discovers they *can't* change the past—it's heartbreaking.
- In The Terminator, the protagonist tries to prevent a future they know is coming—it's desperate.
- In Predestination, the protagonist discovers their identity is paradoxical—it's existentially disturbing.

Don't let mechanics overwhelm theme. Time travel is the vehicle; the human question is the story.

## Common Sci-Fi Pitfalls

### Technobabble

Technobabble is fake scientific language that sounds plausible but means nothing: "the quantum flux capacitor is destabilizing the photonic relay."

Readers (especially readers with scientific backgrounds) notice immediately. If you need pseudo-scientific terms:
- Make them consistent and use them sparingly.
- Use them for flavor, not as plot solution.
- Don't have characters solve plot problems with technobabble explanation.

Better: "The ship's engines are overheating" (clear) than "We're experiencing a catastrophic cascade failure in the dilithium crystal matrix" (sounds impressive, explains nothing).

### Cardboard Characters Serving the Concept

Characters exist to explore the concept, not the reverse. A story about AI consciousness is only interesting if you care about the specific AI (and humans) involved.

Example: A story about an AI that controls a city is boring if the AI is just "the system" with no personality. Make the AI a character with motivations, perspectives, and conflicts.

### Ignoring Social Consequences of Technology

The best sci-fi explores *consequences*. If your worldbuilding includes technology but ignores how it changes society, you're missing the story.

- Virtual reality technology exists → how does it change work, relationships, and mental health?
- Immortality tech exists → how does it change society, overpopulation, power dynamics?
- Mind-reading exists → how does it change privacy, authority, trust?

The technology isn't the story; the *human consequence* is.

### White Room Syndrome

Avoid describing the worldbuilding in isolation. The world exists because characters live in it and interact with it. Show the world through character perspective and action.

## Key Authors and Their Contributions

**Isaac Asimov**: Master of science-driven plots and hard sci-fi concepts. His stories explore the implications of a single technological premise rigorously. Teaches: extrapolation, consistency, and how to make concept serve character.

**Ursula K. Le Guin**: Master of soft sci-fi and anthropological worldbuilding. Her work explores how different social structures and values shape human experience. Teaches: how to use alien worlds as mirrors to examine human assumptions.

**William Gibson**: Pioneer of cyberpunk and cyber-era sci-fi. His prose is layered and dense, and he builds worlds through sensory detail and character perspective. Teaches: immersive worldbuilding, voice, and how technology shapes identity.

**Octavia Butler**: Master of exploring race, gender, and power in speculative futures. Her work is character-driven and emotionally complex. Teaches: how to make speculative premises vehicles for exploring social issues without preaching.

**Andy Weir**: Modern master of problem-solving sci-fi. His work combines hard science with character voice and humor. Teaches: how to make technical detail engaging, how to pace plot around escalating problems, and how to make readers care about survival narratives.

**N.K. Jemisin**: Master of worldbuilding in fantasy and sci-fi. Her work is dense with detail, character-driven, and emotionally complex. Teaches: how to build believable worlds with their own rules and systems.

**Liu Cixin** (Three-Body Problem): Master of hard sci-fi at an epic scale. His work combines physics, philosophy, and existential threat. Teaches: how to make cosmic-scale sci-fi emotionally resonant and how to explore humanity's place in an indifferent universe.

## Science Fiction Craft Checklist

Before finishing your sci-fi manuscript:

- Is your core speculative premise clear? Can you state it in one sentence?
- Have you extrapolated the consequences consistently?
- Do you avoid multiple contradictory speculative premises?
- Is technology serving theme, not obscuring it?
- Are you showing the world through character action, not explaining it via exposition?
- Does the world feel lived-in, not just described?
- Are characters equally developed as the concept?
- Have you addressed the social/philosophical implications of your speculative premise?
- Is your time-travel model (if applicable) consistent?
- Are you avoiding technobabble and info-dumps?
- Does the story answer the human question underneath the sci-fi premise?

If you're answering "no" to multiple questions, your foundation may need strengthening.
