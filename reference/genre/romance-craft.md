# Romance Craft Reference

## The Central Promise: HEA and HFN

Romance is the only genre where the ending is mandated by reader expectations. Your story must deliver one of two things:

- **HEA (Happily Ever After)**: The romantic couple is together, their conflicts resolved, and their future appears permanent and secure.
- **HFN (Happy For Now)**: The couple is together and happy in the moment, but the long-term future remains open. Used when external circumstances prevent a permanent commitment (one character moving away, uncertain job situations, etc.).

Failure to deliver HEA or HFN breaks the fundamental genre contract. Readers choose romance *because* they expect emotional satisfaction at the relationship's core. A "bittersweet" ending or tragic separation, regardless of literary merit, violates the promise that brought readers to the genre.

The HEA/HFN requirement doesn't mean saccharine or simple—your couple can face ongoing challenges, disagreements, or external pressures. But the *relationship* must be intact, committed, and moving forward.

### Why This Matters

Romance readers understand the rules. They know the lovers will end up together. The question isn't *whether* but *how*—and this "how" is where tension and story live. The central promise removes one variable, allowing readers to focus on the emotional journey, the obstacles, and the character transformation that make the ending feel earned.

## Meet Cute: First Impressions That Stick

The initial meeting between protagonists is critical. It must be:

1. **Memorable**: Distinctive enough that readers immediately recall it.
2. **Revealing of character**: What the characters do and say in this moment defines them.
3. **Sparking**: An underlying attraction, tension, or connection (romantic, antagonistic, or uncertain).

### Meet Cute Types

**Collision**: Literal or metaphorical crash. Coffee spilled on expensive clothing, literally bumping into each other, crashing into their life. Physical comedy creates instant intimacy.

**Forced Proximity**: Characters stuck together—snowed in, stranded on a plane, locked in a room, sharing a cabin. They can't escape, so they must engage.

**Mistaken Identity**: One character believes the other is someone else. Creates comedy and vulnerability when the truth emerges.

**Professional Clash**: Attorney vs. judge, teacher vs. administrator, rival performers. Built-in conflict and unequal power dynamics that must shift over time.

**Rescue**: One character saves the other from danger, embarrassment, or crisis. Creates instant emotional debt and gratitude.

**Mutual Friends**: Introduced by a matchmaking friend or at a social gathering. Lower stakes, but the chemistry must be evident immediately to transcend "nice to meet you."

The meet cute is not about love at first sight (though it can be). It's about the *spark*—the moment that makes both character and reader think, "Something just changed." This spark might be attraction, antagonism, curiosity, or recognition. But it must exist.

## Tension and Chemistry: The Engine of Romance

Romance stories run on two types of tension working in concert:

### Sexual Tension

The physical attraction between characters. This doesn't require explicit content (see: heat levels below), but the reader must *feel* that these characters want each other physically. Tension comes from:

- **Proximity**: They're close but can't touch. The almost-kiss, the accidental hand-holding, the brush of shoulders.
- **Avoidance**: They try to stay apart because touching is dangerous.
- **Denial**: They refuse to admit the attraction, but their body language betrays them.
- **Stolen moments**: Brief, stolen physical contact that feels electric because it's forbidden or risky.

Sexual tension escalates throughout the story, moving from accidental touch to intentional proximity to full physical intimacy (at whatever heat level you've chosen).

### Emotional Tension

The internal conflict preventing the characters from being together. This is more important than external obstacles:

- **Fear**: Of vulnerability, of being hurt again, of not being enough, of losing independence.
- **Belief systems**: One character believes love is a liability; another doesn't believe they're worthy of it.
- **Past trauma**: Abandonment, betrayal, or loss that creates protective barriers.
- **Incompatibility of values**: One prioritizes career; another prioritizes family. One wants commitment; another fears it.
- **Competing loyalties**: Duty to family, friendship, or principle vs. love for this person.

Emotional tension is deeper and more lasting than physical attraction. It's what makes readers invest. A couple can have great sexual chemistry but terrible emotional fit—and that mismatch is where real conflict lives.

### The Push-Pull Dynamic

Chemistry manifests as a push-pull dance:

- One character wants closeness; the other needs space.
- One confesses feelings; the other withdraws.
- One moves toward; the other pulls back.
- The intensity shifts, but the pattern repeats, each cycle bringing them slightly closer together and revealing more vulnerability.

This dynamic creates the "almost" moments:

- **Almost-kiss**: They're close, the air is charged, and then... someone pulls away. Interrupted, or one character retreats. The near-miss increases anticipation.
- **Almost-confession**: A character nearly admits their feelings but stops themselves.
- **Almost-intimate moment**: They share something vulnerable or physical, then one character panics and puts walls back up.

Each "almost" is a promise the story will eventually fulfill. Readers savor the delay because they know the payoff is coming.

## Popular Tropes and Their Structural Conventions

### Enemies to Lovers

**Core tension**: Character A and Character B actively dislike or oppose each other. Over time, they discover depth, vulnerability, and attraction.

**Structure**:
1. **Inciting conflict**: Something forces them into each other's space.
2. **Forced collaboration**: They must work together despite disliking each other. Banter is key—witty, sharp dialogue where both characters hold their own.
3. **Slow reveal**: Small moments show character depth beyond the antagonism. What caused the initial clash? Why do they really dislike each other?
4. **Reframing**: A key conversation where they acknowledge the other person's humanity and justify their initial opposition.
5. **Attraction shift**: Now aware of the other's vulnerability and depth, attraction becomes inevitable.
6. **Black moment**: Often, one character's past or insecurity makes them sabotage the relationship, believing they don't deserve this person.

**Key skill**: Make the initial conflict real and meaningful, not contrived. Readers must understand why they couldn't get along initially. The best enemies-to-lovers stories feature characters who push each other toward growth.

### Friends to Lovers

**Core tension**: Deep friendship, genuine care and history, but one or both fear that acknowledging romance will destroy what exists.

**Structure**:
1. **Established friendship**: Show readers why these characters matter to each other. What makes them a great team?
2. **Catalyst**: A moment that shifts the dynamic—one character realizes they want more, or external pressure (another character's interest, changed circumstances) forces the realization.
3. **Denial phase**: The character with feelings hides them. Readers see the internal conflict; the other character remains oblivious.
4. **Tension build**: Small moments reveal the hidden feelings. Jealousy when the friend shows interest in someone else. Lingering touches. Extended eye contact.
5. **Black moment**: Usually, the confession of feelings backfires or threatens the friendship. The character fears losing the friendship entirely.
6. **Resolution**: The other character admits reciprocal feelings, often revealing they've felt the same for longer.

**Key skill**: Make the friendship real enough that the stakes of losing it feel genuine. Readers must worry that romantic feelings will ruin something precious.

### Fake Dating

**Core tension**: Characters pretend to be in a relationship for external reasons (fooling family, making someone jealous, maintaining a business arrangement), but real feelings develop.

**Structure**:
1. **Setup**: Why do they need to fake date? The reason must be compelling enough to justify the deception.
2. **Rules and boundaries**: They establish what the fake relationship will and won't include (public displays, meeting family, etc.).
3. **First fake moments**: Small, innocent moments (holding hands in public, introducing as "boyfriend/girlfriend") feel charged because they're not real—yet.
4. **Rules break**: Physical or emotional boundaries slip. A kiss intended to be convincing feels real. A vulnerable conversation happens.
5. **Escalation**: The fake dating becomes more immersive. Meeting family, declarations of feeling, deepening intimacy.
6. **Exposure threat**: Risk that the deception will be discovered, or the original reason for fake dating is resolved, forcing a decision.
7. **Real confession**: At least one character must admit real feelings and risk rejection.

**Key skill**: Balance comedy and heart. Fake dating works because it's funny (the awkwardness, the close calls), but readers care because real feelings develop. The humor protects emotional vulnerability.

### Second Chance

**Core tension**: Characters have history—they were in love, or committed, or nearly committed. Separation happened. Now they're reunited, and old feelings resurface alongside hurt and doubt.

**Structure**:
1. **History**: Show readers why they were together and why they separated. The separation must have been painful and felt final.
2. **Time jump**: Years pass. Both characters have changed, moved on (or seemed to).
3. **Reunion**: Chance or necessity brings them back together. The initial meeting is charged with unresolved emotion.
4. **Reacquaintance**: They've changed. Are they still compatible? Old patterns might resurface, or they might discover new depth.
5. **Unfinished business**: What caused the separation? Miscommunication? Circumstance? Incompatibility? This must be addressed directly.
6. **Black moment**: Often, the same issue that separated them initially threatens again, forcing a reckoning.
7. **Earned reconciliation**: This ending feels different from a first-time coupling because both characters must *choose* each other again, knowing the risks.

**Key skill**: Make separation feel real and lasting. Second chances are only powerful if readers believed the couple was truly over. Show how both characters have grown and changed to justify trying again.

### Forbidden Love

**Core tension**: External barriers prevent the relationship: class differences, workplace power dynamics, family opposition, betrayal of friendship, legal/moral prohibitions, or cultural/religious divides.

**Structure**:
1. **The prohibition**: Establish clearly why they can't be together. The barrier must be compelling, not arbitrary.
2. **Secret connection**: They find small ways to be together or communicate, heightening the forbidden nature.
3. **Escalation**: The relationship deepens despite (or because of) the prohibition.
4. **Discovery**: The secret is revealed, and consequences follow.
5. **Choice moment**: Both characters must choose each other against the prohibition. This requires sacrifice.
6. **Resolution**: Either the barrier falls away (circumstances change, characters defy social norms), or they choose each other anyway and accept the consequences.

**Key skill**: Make the barrier feel real enough to create genuine conflict. If readers don't understand why they can't be together, the forbidden aspect feels artificial. Modern romance often subverts this trope—the "barrier" is outdated social convention, and the characters' defiance becomes empowering.

### Age Gap

**Core tension**: Significant age difference between characters creates power imbalances, life-stage misalignment, and social judgment.

**Structure**:
1. **Introduction**: Show the age difference plainly. How do they meet? What's the context?
2. **Power dynamics**: Address the elephant in the room. Is one character significantly more experienced? Does one have power over the other professionally or socially?
3. **Genuine connection**: Show that attraction and care transcend the age difference. What do they have in common?
4. **Social friction**: How do others react? Family, friends, society? This creates external pressure.
5. **Black moment**: Often involves self-doubt. Does the younger character fear they're not mature enough? Does the older character worry they're taking advantage?
6. **Earned respect**: The resolution requires mutual respect and equal partnership, not parental care or power imbalance.

**Key skill**: Modern age-gap romance must be careful about power dynamics. If one character is dependent on the other professionally or financially, the relationship feels coercive rather than romantic. The best age-gap romances feature characters who are equals despite the age difference.

### Grumpy/Sunshine

**Core tension**: Personality opposites—one character is cynical, withdrawn, or prickly; the other is optimistic, outgoing, or sunny. They soften each other.

**Structure**:
1. **Introduction**: Establish the personality gap. Show the grumpy character's world (isolated, controlled, cynical) and the sunshine character's world (open, chaotic, hopeful).
2. **Collision**: They're forced into each other's space. The sunshine character cheerfully ignores the grumpy character's attempts to be left alone.
3. **Softening**: Tiny moments reveal the grumpy character's capacity for warmth. A smile. A laugh. Protecting the sunshine character.
4. **Vulnerability**: The grumpy character reveals why they became grumpy—past hurt, loss, betrayal.
5. **Mutual transformation**: The sunshine character learns that positivity isn't blind—real challenges exist. The grumpy character learns that hope isn't naive.
6. **Defenses down**: Both characters become more themselves: grumpy character more openly emotional, sunshine character more honest about pain.

**Key skill**: Avoid making the grumpy character a "project" for the sunshine character. The best grumpy/sunshine romances feature equal transformation. The grumpy character doesn't become sunshine; they become a healthier version of themselves. The sunshine character doesn't dim; they develop realistic boundaries.

### Slow Burn

**Core tension**: Attraction and affection build gradually over time. The relationship takes time to acknowledge, develop, and consummate.

**Structure**:
1. **Early friendship**: Characters meet and connect, but romance isn't immediate.
2. **Gradual awareness**: Over time, small moments accumulate. Longer conversations. Seeking each other's company. Inside jokes.
3. **Unconscious shift**: At some point, one character (or both) realizes the relationship has deepened beyond friendship.
4. **Hesitation**: Acknowledging romance feels risky because the friendship is valuable. Moving forward means risking everything they have.
5. **Slow intensification**: Physical connection builds gradually. A hand held. An accidental kiss. Intentional kissing.
6. **Time investment**: The entire book is the slow burn. Readers savor the development because it's earned.

**Key skill**: Pacing is critical. Slow burn requires strong character voice and internal monologue so readers remain engaged during the long build-up. Quick action beats help maintain momentum. The payoff must feel worth the wait.

## Internal Conflict: Why They Can't Be Together (Yet)

External obstacles alone don't make compelling romance. A couple can be separated by circumstances, but if they have no internal barriers, readers feel cheated. "Just get together already," they'll think.

Real romance tension comes from internal conflict—the reasons characters believe they *shouldn't* or *can't* be together:

### Common Internal Barriers

**Belief Systems**:
- "Love always ends in pain." (Based on parental divorce, betrayal, or loss.)
- "I'm not enough." (Based on past rejection, failure, or internalized shame.)
- "Love is weakness." (Based on cultural messaging or observation that love compromised someone they respect.)
- "I can't afford emotional distraction." (Career, responsibility, or survival is the priority.)
- "People always leave me." (Abandonment trauma.)

**Fear Responses**:
- Fear of vulnerability. Loving someone means risk; they might leave, judge, or reject.
- Fear of responsibility. If they let someone depend on them, they might fail.
- Fear of losing identity. Love might consume them or require compromise they won't accept.
- Fear of repetition. "This always goes the same way, and it ends badly."

**Competing Loyalties**:
- Duty to family or friends conflicts with romantic love.
- Professional ethics (boss/employee, therapist/client, attorney/defendant).
- Friendship loyalty. "If I pursue this, I betray my friend."

**Perceived Incompatibility**:
- Different life goals (one wants children; the other doesn't).
- Different values (money, religion, politics, lifestyle).
- Life stages (one ready to settle; one needs freedom).
- Past patterns or habits that concern the other.

The strength of the internal conflict determines the strength of the story. A character who simply doesn't believe they deserve love will create more compelling tension than a character whose only barrier is geography.

## The Black Moment: All Is Lost

Every romance has a black moment—the point where the relationship appears impossible, where breaking up seems necessary, where readers genuinely worry the HEA is in jeopardy. This moment is essential because without it, the ending feels unearned.

### What Makes a Powerful Black Moment

**Emotional devastation**: The reader must feel the loss. This isn't a misunderstanding easily cleared up; it's a genuine rupture.

**Character flaws on display**: The black moment stems from character weakness, not external misfortune. One character's inability to trust sabotages the relationship. Another's fear of vulnerability causes them to push away. A third's refusal to compromise creates incompatibility.

**Real consequences**: There are lasting effects. Trust is broken. Feelings are hurt. Promises are violated. Something is genuinely damaged.

**Stakes clarified**: The black moment makes clear what the characters stand to lose. Often, readers realize for the first time how much these characters care.

### The Black Moment Is Not

**A misunderstanding**: "He didn't understand why she left" is weak. The black moment requires real conflict, not simple miscommunication.

**Bad luck**: A meteor doesn't strike and kill one character, making the relationship impossible. The barrier must be *because of* who the characters are.

**Third-party interference**: Someone else doesn't separate them. If they break apart, it's because of their own actions or choices.

### After the Black Moment

The black moment forces a reckoning. At least one character must change—not compromise (which implies both give up something), but genuinely transform. They must overcome the internal barrier that caused the black moment.

The resolution isn't that circumstances change; it's that characters change. The external barrier might fall away, but the internal barrier's resolution is what makes the ending satisfying.

## Consent and Healthy Relationships

Modern romance readers expect:

### Enthusiastic Consent

Consent is sexy. Show characters asking for permission, offering reassurance, checking in. "Is this okay?" is intimate, not awkward. Consent isn't a single moment; it's ongoing negotiation.

- Characters say yes enthusiastically, not reluctantly.
- Characters can withdraw consent at any time.
- Explicit communication is valued over "reading signals."
- Characters respect boundaries immediately.

### Power Dynamics Awareness

Be intentional about power imbalances:

**Workplace dynamics**: Boss/employee relationships are problematic because of structural power. If you write this trope, address it directly. Often, the power imbalance must be resolved (one character leaves the job, one is promoted out of direct authority) before the relationship can be healthy.

**Age gaps**: Make clear that the younger character is a fully formed adult, not a "project." Equal voice and agency are essential.

**Wealth disparity**: Money imbalances can create problematic dynamics. Show the less wealthy character maintaining independence and choices.

**Professional relationships**: Doctor/patient, therapist/client relationships have ethical prohibitions for good reason. If you're writing these tropes, address why the prohibition exists and how it's overcome.

### Toxic Behaviors to Avoid Romanticizing

Modern readers reject:

- **Controlling behavior** (what you wear, who you see, where you go) framed as "protective love."
- **Jealousy** as a sign of passion. Jealousy is insecurity; excessive jealousy is controlling.
- **Lack of accountability**: "I cheated, but I love you, so you should forgive me." Love without responsibility and change isn't love.
- **Love as salvation**: One character isn't responsible for fixing the other character's mental health or life problems.
- **Non-negotiable sacrifice**: Love shouldn't require one character to abandon dreams, identity, or values.

Healthy romance involves mutual respect, equal voice, accountability, and genuine care for the other person's well-being—not just romantic feelings.

## Heat Levels: Matching Content to Audience

Romance fiction is marketed by heat level, indicating the explicitness of sexual content:

### Sweet/Clean (Closed Door)

No explicit sexual content on page. Sexual encounters happen off-page or are implied.

- May include kissing, passionate embrace, clothing removal.
- Fade-to-black before sexual activity.
- Audience: Readers who prioritize emotional story over physical intimacy, older readers, readers with religious or personal preferences for clean content.
- Examples: Nicholas Sparks, Debbie Macomber.

### Warm

Sexual content is present but not explicit. Usually includes:

- Kissing and passionate embrace.
- Allusions to sexual activity.
- Fade-to-black before detailed activity.
- Mild language.

Audience: Readers who want sexual content acknowledged but not graphically described.

### Sensual

Sexual content is explicit and described, but not graphic:

- Multiple detailed sex scenes.
- Anatomical terms and specific actions, but not graphic language or degradation.
- Characters' pleasure and sensation are described.
- Audience: Readers who enjoy sexual content and expect multiple scenes throughout the story.

### Spicy

Explicit, graphic sexual content with:

- Detailed descriptions of sexual activity.
- Multiple sex scenes with variation.
- Potentially incorporates BDSM elements, various positions, and explicit language.
- Audience: Readers who prioritize sexual content and expect it frequently.

### Erotic

Sexual content dominates the narrative. Sex is the primary plot element, not a subplot.

- Frequent, very graphic sexual scenes.
- Exploration of sexual desires, kinks, and fantasies.
- Often includes elements like BDSM, voyeurism, or sexual power play.
- Audience: Readers for whom sexual content is the primary appeal.

### Important Note on Heat Levels

Readers research heat level carefully. Mismatching content to label damages trust. If you promise sweet and deliver spicy, readers feel deceived. If you promise spicy and deliver warm, readers feel cheated.

Modern marketing often includes blurb warnings ("This book contains explicit content," "Contains BDSM elements") to ensure readers know what they're getting.

## Dual POV: Both Perspectives Matter

Most contemporary romance uses dual POV (point of view), alternating chapters between the two protagonists. Both characters have interiority—readers see their thoughts, feelings, and motivations.

### Why Dual POV Works for Romance

- Readers understand both sides of the tension simultaneously. When Character A wants to confess feelings, readers know Character B has felt the same for weeks.
- The almost-moments are heightened because readers know both characters' internal conflicts.
- Misunderstandings are more poignant when readers know what each character *thinks* the other feels vs. what they actually feel.
- Both characters feel equally important and equally deserving of the HEA.

### Dual POV Challenges

**Unequal development**: One POV character must be as compelling as the other. If one character's chapters are shorter, less detailed, or less introspective, they feel secondary.

**Redundancy**: Avoid covering the same scene identically in both POVs. When the scene repeats immediately, momentum stalls. Vary what each POV character noticed, thought, and felt.

**Voice distinction**: Each POV character should have a distinct voice—vocabulary, sentence structure, perspective. If both sound identical, readers won't be able to tell them apart without name tags.

**Balance**: Roughly equal page time between POVs. Significantly favoring one character suggests they're the "real" protagonist.

### Single POV Romance

Some romance uses a single POV (usually the character with the most to learn). This works when:

- The mystery of the other character's feelings creates tension.
- The reader wonders alongside the POV character whether their feelings are reciprocated.

Single POV romance requires more subtle communication of the other character's feelings through dialogue, body language, and actions, since readers can't access their thoughts.

## Romance Subgenres

### Contemporary Romance

Set in the current time period, reflecting modern mores and contexts:

- Relationships develop in present-day settings (cities, small towns, workplaces).
- Technology and modern communication play roles.
- Themes reflect current social issues (work/life balance, online dating, family expectations).
- Audience expects realism and relatability.

**Key considerations**: Modern relationship challenges are different from historical ones. Contemporary romance must address modern consent, communication, and logistics.

### Historical Romance

Set before World War II, typically with attention to period-accurate details:

- Regency-era romance (1811-1820s) is exceptionally popular, featuring balls, estates, and social hierarchies.
- Victorian, Edwardian, medieval, and American frontier historical romance all have strong readerships.
- Period-accurate dialogue, clothing, customs, and social rules matter.

**Key considerations**: Research accuracy affects credibility. Readers are often experts on their preferred historical period and will notice errors. Balance period authenticity with modern audience expectations (modern romance readers don't accept "women had no agency" as plot justification).

### Paranormal Romance

Incorporates fantastical, supernatural, or speculative elements:

- Vampire romance, werewolf/shifter romance, ghost romance, magic/witches.
- Paranormal romance world-building includes rules for how the paranormal element functions.
- Often includes paranormal world politics, hidden communities, or human-paranormal relationships.

**Key consideration**: The paranormal element must be integral to the story, not window dressing. If you removed the paranormal aspect, does the story still work? If yes, it's not truly paranormal romance.

### Romantic Suspense

Combines romance with mystery or thriller elements:

- The couple must work together to solve a crime, survive danger, or overcome a threat.
- The romantic plot and suspense plot are equally weighted.
- Trust and vulnerability deepen as they navigate danger together.

**Key consideration**: Both plots must be compelling. Weak suspense feels like a tacked-on excuse for the couple to spend time together. Weak romance feels like a distraction from the thriller.

### Rom-Com

Emphasizes humor and romantic comedy:

- Situation comedy drives the plot alongside relationship development.
- Witty dialogue, absurd situations, and comedic timing are essential.
- Emotional depth balanced with humor; characters aren't jokes, even if situations are funny.

**Key consideration**: Humor is subjective. What's hilarious to one reader falls flat for another. Rom-com requires confident voice and timing.

### Dark Romance

Explores darker themes, morally gray characters, and edgier content:

- Characters may be antiheroes, criminals, or ethically compromised.
- Themes may include violence, trauma, organized crime, or power imbalances.
- The romance is often possessive, obsessive, or marked by power dynamics.

**Key consideration**: Modern dark romance must differentiate between "morally complex character" and "character doing actually harmful things that should be addressed." The line between dark romance and romanticizing abuse is delicate. Readers expect accountability and growth, not celebration of harmful behavior.

## Key Authors and What They Teach

Understanding craft means studying masters:

**Nora Roberts**: Prolific romance pioneer. Master of plot mechanics, multiple storylines, and character development. Her work demonstrates how to balance romance with plot complications without losing focus on the relationship.

**Julia Quinn**: Master of wit, banter, and humor. Her Bridgerton series shows how to make archetypal characters (lord, lady, etc.) feel fresh through voice and personality. Strong in dialogue craft.

**Talia Hibbert**: Modern romance centered on Black characters and relationships. Strong emotional depth, complex family dynamics, and balance of steam and heart. Teaches voice, representation, and emotional authenticity.

**Casey McQuiston**: Contemporary romance with political/social elements. LGBTQ+ representation, humor, and high-stakes plotting. Demonstrates how identity and history inform relationship dynamics.

**Lisa Kleypas**: Master of emotional depth and sensuality. Her work shows how to write romantic tension that builds throughout an entire novel. Strong in internal conflict and character transformation.

**Helen Hoang**: Master of consent, communication, and disability representation. Her work prioritizes explicit conversation between characters and demonstrates how consent can be intimate and sexy.

## Romance Fundamentals Checklist

Before finishing your romance manuscript:

- Do your protagonists have HEA or HFN? (Non-negotiable.)
- Does the meet-cute spark character and reader interest?
- Is there consistent sexual and emotional tension building throughout?
- Does at least one character have a compelling internal conflict preventing the relationship?
- Is the black moment genuinely devastating and rooted in character flaws?
- Do both characters grow and change by the end?
- Are power dynamics (if present) addressed intentionally?
- Is consent explicit and enthusiastic?
- Do both POV characters (if dual POV) feel equally developed?
- Does the romance feel earned, not arbitrary?

If you're answering "no" to multiple questions, the romance foundation may need strengthening.
