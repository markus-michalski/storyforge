# LGBTQ+ Fiction Craft Reference

## Introduction: Representing Queerness Authentically

LGBTQ+ fiction encompasses stories featuring lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, nonbinary, and queer characters. The craft challenges are distinct from other genres because they involve not only storytelling technique but also representation, authenticity, and navigating a complex conversation about whose stories get told and how.

This document is not about whether non-queer people should write queer characters (they can, with research and sensitivity) but about what makes representation authentic and stories powerful. It's about recognizing and avoiding harmful patterns while creating space for queer joy, complexity, and humanity.

---

## 1. Authentic Representation: Queerness as Lived Experience

### Three-Dimensional Queer Characters

The first principle: queer characters are *people*, not identity labels.

A queer character should be as complex, contradictory, and fully realized as any character. They should have:

- Goals and ambitions unrelated to sexuality or gender (career, art, knowledge, adventure)
- Relationships beyond romantic (family, friendship, mentorship, rivalry)
- Flaws and moral complexity
- Internal conflicts unrelated to being queer
- A life that extends beyond the reader's view

Being queer is *one facet* of a person's identity, not the entire identity. Just as a straight character's heterosexuality is background information, not their defining trait, queerness should be integrated into a fuller characterization.

### Avoiding Tokenism

Tokenism occurs when a character is included for diversity without genuine development or integration into the narrative. The character feels like a box checked: "I have a gay character."

**Signs of tokenism:**
- The queer character exists primarily to serve straight characters' development (the dead lesbian who motivates the straight protagonist)
- The character's only trait is their queerness
- They exist at the periphery with minimal agency
- Their storyline is resolved or ignored to make space for "main" characters
- They fulfill stereotypes rather than subverting them

To avoid tokenism, ask: If I removed this character's queer identity, would they still be necessary to the story? Do they have their own goals, conflicts, and arc? Would their death/departure genuinely impact the narrative?

### Integration into Narrative

A queer character should be *integrated* into the story's fabric, not added on. Their queerness affects:

- Their relationships and social position
- Their choices and conflicts
- Their perspective on the world
- Their internal experience

But it shouldn't be the *only* thing that affects these. A character might avoid coming out because of legitimate danger, but they also might avoid it because they're private, because they're unsure of their identity, because they're prioritizing other things, or for complex psychological reasons.

## 2. Coming Out Stories: When They Matter, When They're Overdone

### The Power of Coming Out Narratives

Coming out stories can be powerful: they explore identity formation, fear, acceptance, rejection, liberation. They examine how we present ourselves to the world and what it costs to be authentic.

**Coming out matters when:**
- The story explores the emotional reality of hiding and revelation
- The character's safety or relationships genuinely hang in the balance
- The narrative genuinely grapples with religious faith, family dynamics, or cultural context
- The story examines *how* someone comes out, not just *that* they do
- It's central to the character's arc and thematic concerns

### Post-Coming-Out Narratives

Not every queer story needs to be *about* coming out. Contemporary readers, particularly younger readers, increasingly want stories that *begin* after coming out.

Post-coming-out narratives explore:

- Navigating relationships post-revelation
- Finding community and belonging
- Dating and romance
- Life goals and ambitions
- Internal identity questions unrelated to others' acceptance
- Complex family dynamics post-acceptance

These stories treat queerness as background (integrated) rather than foreground (primary plot driver).

**Example:** In *One Last Stop* by Casey McQuiston, the character is already out at the story's beginning. The narrative focuses on unexpected love and connection, not the anxiety of revelation.

### The Overdone Coming Out Problem

Readers have encountered countless coming-out stories. If your story is coming-out focused, ask: Does mine have something new to say? Do I explore psychological or relational dimensions that haven't been thoroughly examined?

Coming-out stories that feel fresh often:
- Explore specific cultural contexts (a person coming out within a religious community, within a specific cultural tradition)
- Focus on an unexpected relationship dynamic (coming out to a parent you've been estranged from)
- Examine coming out as an ongoing process (not a single moment but iterative)
- Show multiple perspectives (how does this affect different family members differently?)

## 3. "Bury Your Gays": Avoiding Harmful Tropes

### The Dead Lesbian Syndrome

"Bury Your Gays" is a historical pattern in media where LGBTQ+ characters face disproportionately tragic fates—particularly death—often to advance straight characters' narratives.

**The pattern:**
- A lesbian character falls in love or finds happiness, then dies to motivate the straight protagonist
- A gay character's death is treated as acceptable/inevitable tragedy
- Queer characters die at higher rates than straight characters
- The queer character's death doesn't significantly impact the queer community but devastates a straight character

This pattern reflects historical censorship: in eras when happy queer endings were prohibited, queer characters were killed off. That history is now baked into narrative convention.

### Modern Context

Contemporary readers are attuned to this pattern and respond negatively to it. A queer character's death now reads as lazy storytelling—relying on a tired pattern rather than crafting meaningful narrative.

**Key principle:** If a queer character dies, their death should be:

- **Narratively justified**: It serves the overall story, not just shock value.
- **Emotionally grounded**: We understand what it costs, not just for straight characters but for the queer community/family.
- **Avoided if unnecessary**: If the story doesn't *require* this death, cut it. A queer character surviving doesn't weaken your narrative.
- **Treated with significance**: Don't use a queer character's death as a plot device without exploring its ramifications.

### The Flip Side: Queer Joy and Happy Endings

The antidote to "Bury Your Gays" is not just avoiding death but embracing queer joy and happy endings.

Queer joy means:

- Characters experiencing genuine happiness, not just accepting their fate
- Community, celebration, laughter, and love
- Romance that doesn't resolve in tragedy
- Characters thriving, not just surviving
- Celebration of queer life, not just tragedy

This doesn't mean every story needs a romance or a happy ending. But ensuring that *some* queer stories are happy, joyful, and celebratory is important for representation.

## 4. Queerness as Normal vs. Queerness as Theme

### When Queerness Is Incidental (Queer Joy, Queer Adventure)

These stories treat being queer as normal, integrated, and unexceptional. The character's sexuality or gender is mentioned naturally but isn't the driving thematic concern.

**Example:** A heist novel where the protagonist is a lesbian. The queerness is *there*—it affects relationships, perspectives—but the story is about pulling off a heist. The queerness doesn't require explanation or focus.

These stories are increasingly valued because they normalize queerness. They allow queer readers to see themselves in stories where queerness isn't the problem to be solved.

**When to write this:** When you want to tell a story that happens to feature queer characters. When you're interested in genres (heist, fantasy, thriller) that feature queer people.

### When Queerness Is Central Theme

These stories make queerness the primary thematic focus: identity formation, discrimination, liberation, community, hidden history.

**Example:** A novel about a transgender person navigating transition—their internal experience, their relationships, their place in the world.

These stories require deeper engagement with queer experience. They demand authenticity because readers can assess accuracy against their own experience or that of people they know.

**When to write this:** When you have genuine insight into queer experience and something meaningful to explore about identity, discrimination, or belonging.

### Both Approaches Are Valid

Neither approach is better. Both serve the goal of representing queer people in fiction. The distinction matters because it affects how you approach the story.

A queer heist novel doesn't need extensive exploration of the character's coming-out trauma if that's not the story's focus. A novel about gender transition should engage seriously with that experience.

## 5. Intersectionality: Queerness + Other Identities

### The Problem of Monolithic Queerness

"Queer" is an umbrella term containing enormous diversity. LGBTQ+ people are also racialized, classed, disabled, religious, immigrant, etc. The intersection of these identities creates different lived experiences.

A white, middle-class, gay man's experience differs significantly from a Black, working-class, trans woman's experience. Both are queer, but "queer experience" is not monolithic.

### Specificity Over Generality

Rather than writing "a queer character," consider:

- What specific sexuality or gender identity?
- What race/ethnicity?
- What class background?
- What other marginalized or privileged identities?
- How do these intersect to create a specific lived experience?

A Black lesbian navigates different discrimination, community, family dynamics, and cultural contexts than a white lesbian. A disabled gay man faces different accessibility questions. A trans immigrant faces distinct legal and social challenges.

**Principle:** Specificity makes characters richer and representation more authentic. Generalizing "queerness" ironically makes characters less real.

### Centering Marginalized Voices

Contemporary LGBTQ+ fiction is increasingly centering voices from the most marginalized within the community:

- Trans people, especially trans people of color
- Queer disabled people
- Queer immigrant and refugee communities
- Low-income queer communities
- Queer people in non-Western contexts

If you're writing outside your lived experience, research and sensitivity are crucial. But also recognize that centering marginalized voices doesn't mean straight people or privileged queers should never write such characters—it means we should actively platform stories from people with these lived experiences.

### Avoiding Tokenism Through Intersectionality

A character of color who is also queer is not a token diversity addition if:

- Their racialized identity is fully explored, not stereotyped
- Their queer identity is fully explored, not stereotyped
- The intersection creates unique experience (not just additive)
- They're not reduced to a single marginalized identity
- They have complexity, agency, and goals

## 6. Romance Specifics: M/M, F/F, Non-Binary Romance

### Different Relationship Dynamics

Romance dynamics differ based on genders/identities involved:

- **M/M romance**: Historically flourished in fan spaces; now increasingly mainstream. Questions about power dynamics, vulnerability, intimacy between men (culturally taught as inexpressive).
- **F/F romance**: Often explores intimacy, desire, tenderness; culturally coded as "safer" (which creates its own problems); increasingly reclaiming passion and desire.
- **Non-binary romance**: Often explores desire outside gender categories; questions about what attraction means; intimate connection without gendered expectations.

None of these should be *formulaic*. Each relationship is unique to the individuals involved.

### Avoiding Heteronormative Templates

A common error: writing queer romance by inserting same-gender characters into heterosexual romance templates.

**Example of heteronormative template**: One character takes the "active" role (pursuer, protector, top), one takes the "passive" role (pursued, protected, bottom). This mirrors traditional gender roles and reduces complex people to static positions.

**Better approach:** Develop relationship dynamics specific to these characters. What do *they* want? How do *they* negotiate vulnerability, desire, and power?

### Power Dynamics in Queer Relationships

Queer relationships involve diverse power dynamics:

- Equal partners
- Caregiver/care-receiver (when disability is involved)
- Mentor/mentee dynamics
- Age-gap relationships with power imbalances
- Economic imbalances

These should be explored with the same nuance as straight relationships. If power imbalance enables abuse, address it. If the characters navigate it consciously, show that work.

### The "Own Voices" Conversation

"Own Voices" originally meant LGBTQ+ authors writing LGBTQ+ characters (your voice, your community). The concept has become complicated because:

- It can exclude skilled writers from outside the community
- What counts as "own voices" is contested (bisexual authors writing about asexual characters, etc.)
- It can oversimplify authorship

**Current consensus:** Writers should be honest about their identity and perspective. If you're writing outside your experience, do research, consult sensitivity readers, and acknowledge your positionality. If you're writing from experience, that authority matters.

Most importantly: Create space for queer authors' stories while not gatekeeping queer fiction writing.

## 7. Sensitivity Considerations: Language and Representation

### Deadnaming and Misgendering

**Deadnaming**: Using a transgender person's former name without consent.
**Misgendering**: Using incorrect pronouns or gendered language.

**In narrative:**

- If a trans character's deadname is relevant to the story (hiding a past identity, legal papers, etc.), you can use it, but acknowledge the harm and pain it causes.
- If it's not relevant, don't include it. There's no reason readers need to know a character's former name.
- Misgendering by the narrative (not by a character deliberately being cruel) is generally a mistake. Narrate using the character's correct pronouns.
- If a character deliberately misgenders as an act of cruelty, show that it's cruelty. Don't normalize it.

### Transition Narratives: Respectful Representation

If your story includes a character's transition:

- Transition is not the character's entire story or only character trait
- Transition can be joyful, painful, complex—allow multiple emotional responses
- Medical transition details don't need to be voyeuristic (readers don't need graphic detail unless it serves the story)
- Transition is ongoing (gender euphoria/dysphoria is not a one-time plot point)
- Characters can have complicated relationships with their bodies without self-hatred

**Sensitivity:** Consult with trans people, especially trans writers, if exploring transition narratively. Authenticity matters, and trans communities can identify inauthenticity quickly.

### Research and Consultation

If writing outside your experience:

- Research historical and contemporary queer experience
- Read widely within the community (memoirs, fiction, essays)
- If possible, consult sensitivity readers from the community
- Acknowledge when you don't know something rather than guessing
- Be open to feedback and revision

Research prevents common errors: outdated terminology, misunderstanding of experiences, stereotypes presented as fact.

## 8. Stereotypes to Avoid (Or Subvert Meaningfully)

### The Predatory Bisexual

**Stereotype:** Bisexual people are inherently unfaithful, indiscriminate, or predatory.

**Reality:** Bisexuality is attraction to multiple genders; it says nothing about faithfulness or behavior. Some bisexual people are monogamous, some aren't (like all people).

**Avoidance:** If your bisexual character is unfaithful, that's a character choice, not a function of bisexuality. Show fidelity and commitment among bisexual characters too.

### The Tragic Queer

**Stereotype:** Queer characters' lives are tragic; queerness = suffering.

**Reality:** Queer people experience joy, love, community, humor, and happiness.

**Avoidance:** Include queer happiness. Not every queer story is about tragedy or persecution. Queer joy is radical and revolutionary.

### The Sexless Ace/Asexual Character

**Stereotype:** Asexual people are broken, uninterested in relationships, or have nothing interesting to offer.

**Reality:** Asexuality is spectrum. Many ace people have rich romantic lives; many have complex sexuality that doesn't center sexual attraction. Ace people are full humans with desires, ambitions, and desires.

**Avoidance:** Ace characters can have deep romance, passion, and sexuality. Asexuality is not broken heterosexuality.

### The Flamboyant Best Friend

**Stereotype:** The gay best friend exists to support the straight protagonist; he's sassy, funny, sexless, and lacks depth.

**Reality:** Gay men are full people with their own stories, desires, and agency.

**Avoidance:** If a character is queer, give them real narrative presence. Their role isn't to support straight people.

### The Butch Lesbian

**Stereotype:** All butch lesbians are angry, masculine-to-the-point-of-masculine-identity, or traumatized.

**Reality:** Butch lesbians are diverse in personality, presentation, and experience. Butchness is gender expression, not personality type.

**Avoidance:** Create butch characters with full personality ranges. Butchness ≠ anger or trauma (though some butch people certainly experience both).

## 9. Genre Integration: Queer Characters Across Genres

### LGBTQ+ Fantasy

Fantasy offers opportunities to design non-heteronormative worlds:

- **Diverse gender systems**: Three-gender societies, gender-fluid cultures, cultures without rigid gender categories.
- **Magic and identity**: Magic systems tied to gender expression or sexuality; coming-of-age through magical awakening that's also identity formation.
- **Chosen family**: Fantasy's emphasis on found family aligns naturally with queer experience.
- **Power and representation**: Kingdoms with queer monarchs, queer-majority societies, or explicit examination of heteronormativity in worldbuilding.

**Example:** In *Ninth House* by Leigh Bardugo, queer characters exist throughout the Yale setting, their queerness integrated into a magical worldbuilding.

### LGBTQ+ Science Fiction

Sci-fi explores post-gender, post-sexuality futures or examines queerness through speculative lens:

- **Post-gender societies**: What if gender wasn't a primary organizing principle?
- **Non-binary futures**: Exploring gender diversity as norm.
- **Queer futurism**: Imagining liberation and freedom specifically through queer possibility.
- **Technology and identity**: How might technology affect gender expression, sexuality, identity formation?

**Example:** In *Ancillary Justice* by Ann Leckie, the main character uses she/her pronouns for everyone regardless of gender, creating a post-gender linguistic space.

### LGBTQ+ Horror and Dark Fiction

Horror can explore:

- **Queer fear**: Specific fears relevant to queer experience (exposure, isolation, family rejection).
- **Body horror and identity**: Transformation, change, loss of bodily autonomy as metaphor for identity threats.
- **Monstrosity and queerness**: Monsters as metaphor for those society deems unnatural or wrong.

**Example:** In *The Haunting of Hill House*, Shirley Jackson explores Eleanor's queer desire subtly; the house's supernatural danger parallels her internal danger (being consumed by her own desires/isolation).

### LGBTQ+ Mystery and Thriller

Crime fiction with queer protagonists explores:

- **Queer detectives**: How does queer identity affect how they investigate, relate to witnesses, navigate power?
- **Queer crime and punishment**: How does the justice system treat queer victims/perpetrators?
- **Queer communities under threat**: Using the mystery structure to explore community vulnerability or resilience.

**Example:** In *Fingersmith* by Sarah Waters, queer desire drives the plot; the mystery unfolds through queer women's relationships and deception.

### LGBTQ+ Romance

Romance is increasingly diverse:

- **M/M romance**: Now massive mainstream market; ranging from contemporary to paranormal to historical.
- **F/F romance**: Growing market; from contemporary to fantasy to literary.
- **Non-binary romance**: Smaller market but growing; exploring intimacy outside gender binaries.

All romance subgenres can feature queer couples. Don't assume romance = straight.

## 10. Key Authors and Their Contributions

### Casey McQuiston: Joyful Romance and Found Family

McQuiston writes queer romance centered on joy, humor, found family, and happy endings. Her work demonstrates that queer love stories can be celebratory without minimizing real challenges.

**Key contribution:** Proving that queer romance could be mainstream, joyful, and emotionally resonant. Her work centers queer happiness as not just acceptable but desirable.

### TJ Klune: Found Family Fantasy

Klune writes fantasy and paranormal fiction foregrounding found family, often featuring queer characters and relationships. His work combines emotional depth with speculative worldbuilding.

**Key contribution:** Showing how found family—central to much queer experience—could be the emotional heart of epic fantasy. Demonstrating that queer relationships could anchor large-scale narratives.

### Carmen Maria Machado: Literary Horror and Queerness

Machado writes experimental, literary work exploring queerness, desire, identity, and horror. Her work refuses categories and embraces formal innovation.

**Key contribution:** Demonstrating that queer literary fiction could be experimental, intellectually rigorous, and formally inventive. Her work expands what queer fiction can be.

### Becky Albertalli: Contemporary Queer YA

Albertalli writes young adult fiction featuring queer characters navigating contemporary adolescence. Her work is romantic, humorous, and emotionally true.

**Key contribution:** Bringing queer YA to mainstream attention; showing young readers they could see themselves in contemporary, relatable narratives.

### Rivers Solomon: Speculative Queerness

Solomon writes speculative fiction exploring queerness, Black identity, disability, and imagination. Their work is formally innovative and thematically rich.

**Key contribution:** Creating speculative futures centered on queer possibility and imagination; merging Black speculative fiction with queer narratives.

### Hanya Yanagihara: Literary Complexity

Yanagihara's literary fiction explores queer desire, identity, and connection with depth and formal sophistication. Her work treats queer relationships with the same novelistic attention as any literary relationship.

**Key contribution:** Demonstrating that queer literary fiction could achieve mainstream critical acclaim; that literary complexity and queer narratives could coexist without compromise.

## Practical Guidance for Writing LGBTQ+ Fiction

### Start with Character, Not Identity

Develop your character fully before deciding (or remembering) that they're queer. Let their sexuality or gender be integrated into a fuller characterization, not the defining trait.

### Read Widely in the Community

Read contemporary LGBTQ+ fiction, classics, memoirs, essays. Understand the conversation happening within the community. This research prevents stereotypes and deepens authenticity.

### Consider Your Positionality

Are you writing from your own experience? From research and imagination? Be honest about this. It affects your responsibility to accuracy and community.

### Avoid Inspiration Porn

Don't write queer characters overcoming their queerness as inspiration for straight readers. Write queer people *being* queer, living, loving, struggling, and thriving in queerness.

### Center Queer Joy

Ensure some of your queer characters experience joy, love, success, and happiness. Not tragedy, persecution, and overcoming—those are important too, but representation should be diverse.

### Test for Authenticity

If writing outside your experience, have sensitivity readers from the community review. Real feedback from people with lived experience is invaluable.

### Don't Apologize

Write queer characters with confidence. They deserve the same narrative complexity, depth, and care as any character. Don't feel you need to justify their queerness or center straight characters' perspectives on it.
