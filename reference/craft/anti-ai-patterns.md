# Anti-AI Patterns: A Writer's Guide to Authentic Fiction

When artificial intelligence generates narrative, it leaves footprints. These aren't accidents—they're artifacts of how language models work. They process statistical probability rather than human emotion. They optimize for coherence over character. They speak in the voice of the internet average.

This guide identifies the patterns that make AI-generated fiction identifiable and provides countermeasures to ensure your writing sounds authentically human.

## 1. Known AI Tells — Vocabulary

AI systems learn by absorbing vast text corpora. They discover which words and phrases appear frequently in "good writing" and deploy them reflexively. The result: a vocabulary that reads as polished but hollow.

### Heavily Flagged Words and Phrases (AI Tell Indicators)

The following terms appear with suspicious frequency in AI-generated fiction and narrative:

1. **Delve** / **Delve into** — One of the most notorious AI tells. Humans rarely "delve"; they investigate, examine, or explore.
2. **Tapestry** (metaphorical) — "A rich tapestry of..." Almost never used by humans in this way. Overused in AI summaries.
3. **Nuanced** / **Nuance** — AI loves claiming things are nuanced when it needs to sound sophisticated without committing to analysis.
4. **Vibrant** — Favored descriptor for colors, communities, markets. Humans use more specific color words.
5. **Landscape** (metaphorical) — "The technological landscape" or "political landscape." AI's favorite abstraction.
6. **Embark** / **Embark on** — Overly formal and archaic. Humans say "start" or "begin."
7. **Resonate** — "This idea resonates..." AI's go-to word when an emotional connection should be shown, not stated.
8. **Pivotal** — Overused intensifier. Humans prefer "crucial," "turning point," or "critical."
9. **Multifaceted** — Sounds sophisticated but is often meaningless filler.
10. **Realm** — AI's preferred term for domains, fields, or areas. Humans use these simpler words.
11. **Testament** — As in "a testament to..." Formal and generic.
12. **Intricate** — When describing systems or plots. Humans show intricacy; they don't announce it.
13. **Myriad** — Fancy plural. Humans say "many" or "numerous."
14. **Unprecedented** — Hyperbolic placeholder. Real writers ground claims in specifics.
15. **Foster** — As in "foster innovation" or "foster understanding." Awkward in fiction.
16. **Navigate** (metaphorical) — "Navigate the complexities..." Overextended metaphor.
17. **Uncover** — "Uncover the truth." Passive and overused.
18. **Ever-evolving** — Hyphenated placeholder for "changing." Real writers specify what changed.
19. **Beacon** — As a metaphor for hope, guidance, inspiration. Universally flagged in AI content.
20. **Juxtaposition** — When AI needs to sound analytical. Humans show contrast without naming it.
21. **Paradigm** — Almost always signals AI. Real writers avoid the word.
22. **Synergy** — Corporate-speak that shouldn't exist in fiction.
23. **Interplay** — As in "interplay of forces." Vague abstraction.
24. **Shed light on** — AI's preferred synonym for "clarify" or "explain."
25. **Facilitate** — Too formal for narrative fiction.
26. **Streamline** — More AI corporate jargon.
27. **Bolster** — Formal and distant from human speech.
28. **Differentiate** — Academic and didactic.
29. **Refine** — Too gentle for dramatic prose.
30. **Illuminate** — Overused metaphorical explanation.
31. **Harness** — "Harness the power of..." Corporate jargon.
32. **At its core** — Transition favorite of AI, signaling summary-mode.
33. **To put it simply** — Condescending filler that distances the reader.
34. **This underscores the importance of** — Pure AI announce-and-tell.
35. **A key takeaway is** — Corporate voice bleeding into fiction.
36. **From a broader perspective** — Hedging and distancing.
37. **Cutting-edge** — Hyphenated buzzword. Humans describe innovation specifically.
38. **Innovative** / **Innovation** — When used as a generic positive. Humans commit to specifics.
39. **Transformative** — Another hyperbolic placeholder.
40. **Game-changing** — Hyphenated marketing-speak.
41. **Seamless integration** — No human character thinks or speaks this way.
42. **Scalable solution** — Pure business jargon in narrative.
43. **Rich** (as in "rich with meaning") — Overused intensifier. Show the richness.
44. **Journey** (metaphorical) — "A journey toward self-discovery." Humans use "path," "process," or show the actual journey.
45. **Profound** — When attached to vague concepts. AI's favorite complexity-signal.
46. **Compelling** — Meta-commentary. Show why something is compelling.
47. **Dynamic** — Vague intensifier. Describe what actually moves.
48. **Compelling narrative** — Self-aware corporate phrase.
49. **Embrace** (metaphorical) — "Embrace this opportunity." Distant and formal.
50. **In essence** — AI summary-mode indicator.
51. **Remarkably** — Weak intensifier that actually weakens the claim.
52. **Arguably** — Hedging word that signals uncertainty and AI-caution.
53. **Broadly speaking** — Distancing and generalizing.
54. **To some extent** — Hedging that kills commitment.
55. **Generally speaking** — AI's way of saying "I'm not specific."

### Why These Words Signal AI

These words cluster because:

1. **They're abundant in training data** — They appear frequently in published writing, self-help, business writing, and academia.
2. **They sound sophisticated** — But they're euphemisms for showing and demonstrating. Instead of showing nuance, AI announces it.
3. **They hedge commitment** — They let AI avoid specificity, which is essential to human voice.
4. **They're formal and distant** — Human fiction (especially dialogue) uses casual, direct language.
5. **They're abstract** — They name concepts without embodying them. Real writing embodies.

## 2. Known AI Tells — Structure

Structure is where AI's mechanistic thinking becomes most obvious. Humans write with rhythm and breath. AI optimizes for even distribution.

### Structural Tells

**Uniform Sentence Length Distribution**

AI-generated text clusters around 18–22 words per sentence. This is statistically detectable. Human writing has wild variance—a 3-word sentence followed by a 47-word sentence, then a fragment.

*AI Example:*
> The city was bustling with energy and movement. People walked through the streets with purpose and determination. The shops displayed their wares in colorful windows. Everything seemed to move in perfect harmony.

*Human Example:*
> The city pushed. People. Everywhere. And underneath it—that sound, like a crowd held its breath.

**Perfectly Balanced Paragraphs**

AI tends to write paragraphs of nearly identical length. Three sentences each. Four sentences each. Like metronome ticks. Real writers vary paragraph length to control pacing. Short paragraphs for impact. Long paragraphs for immersion.

**Lists of Three (The Triadic Tendency)**

AI loves triads:
- "He was brave, intelligent, and kind."
- "The room was dark, cold, and unwelcoming."
- "She felt fear, anger, and confusion."

This isn't accidental. Language models are trained on vast corpora where triadic structures appear frequently. Humans use twos, fours, fives. Humans break the pattern.

**Every Paragraph Identical in Length**

Scan an AI passage. Count the sentences in each paragraph. Remarkable consistency. Real writing shows variation. Some paragraphs are one sentence. Some are eight.

**Predictable Topic-Sentence → Elaboration → Conclusion Pattern**

Every AI paragraph follows this:
1. Topic sentence (the claim)
2. Supporting sentence (evidence or detail)
3. Elaboration (more evidence)
4. Conclusion (restatement of claim)

Real writers introduce ideas mid-paragraph. They loop back. They digress and return. They break the formula.

**Too-Clean Transitions**

AI overuses transitional phrases:
- "Furthermore"
- "Moreover"
- "In addition"
- "Conversely"
- "On the other hand"

These appear with mechanical frequency. Real writers transition through action, dialogue, or implicit connection. Sometimes they don't transition at all—they just move to the next scene.

## 3. Known AI Tells — Content

Beyond vocabulary and structure, AI content betrays itself through *what it chooses to depict*.

### Generic Descriptions

AI loves empty intensifiers applied to universal concepts:

- "The bustling city"
- "The serene landscape"
- "The vibrant community"
- "The harsh reality"

These descriptions could apply to any city, landscape, or community. Real writers provide *specific, irreducible details*. Not "a vibrant marketplace" but "the butcher's stall where she knew the owner's name, where he always set aside the good cuts."

### Hedging Language

AI constantly softens its claims:
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "In many cases..."
- "To some degree..."
- "Generally speaking..."
- "One might argue..."

This protects AI from falsity. But in fiction, hedging kills conviction. Humans stake claims and own them.

### Overly Balanced Viewpoints

AI avoids controversy. When presented with opposing views, it presents both "fairly." But real writing commits. A character argues their position. A narrator has a perspective. Real fiction is inherently partisan—it takes a side, even if that side is morally gray.

### Lack of Genuine Opinion

AI can simulate opinions but doesn't hold them. So AI-generated fiction lacks *perspective*. It presents events without comment, character without judgment. Real writers embed their perspectives into narration and character voice.

### Telling Emotions Instead of Showing

Rather than showing emotion through action and dialogue:

*AI:*
> She felt a wave of sadness wash over her. Her heart ached with longing. He was overwhelmed by anger.

*Human:*
> She sat on the curb for an hour. Didn't move. When he found her, she was still staring at the same spot on the sidewalk.

### Purple Prose on Demand

When asked for "lyrical" writing, AI becomes overwrought:

> The luminescent tendrils of dawn caressed the verdant meadows with honeyed effulgence, painting the world in aureate splendor.

This isn't lyrical. It's decorated. Real lyrical prose has restraint. One perfect image, not a cascade of adjectives.

## 4. Known AI Tells — Dialogue

Dialogue is where AI's limitations become undeniable.

### All Characters Sound Identical

Every character speaks with the same vocabulary, syntax, and formality. A truck driver and a philosophy professor sound interchangeable. No regional accent, no verbal tics, no class markers in speech.

### Overly Articulate Emotional Speeches

When a character needs to be emotional, AI writes speeches:

> "I need you to understand how much this means to me. The depth of my feelings cannot be adequately expressed in words, yet I must try. You are the fulcrum upon which my happiness pivots."

No real person speaks this way when they're vulnerable. They stammer. They use fragments. They cry.

### No Interruptions

AI dialogue is turn-based. One character speaks. Another responds. Completely. Real dialogue has interruptions, overlaps, unfinished thoughts.

*AI:*
> "I think we should leave."
> "I agree. This situation is untenable."

*Human:*
> "I think we should—"
> "Leave. Yeah. I know."

### No Fragments

Real dialogue is fragmentary. People speak in shorthand. They elide words. They repeat. AI dialogue is grammatically perfect.

> "I don't know. You just... I can't."

AI wouldn't write this. Too incomplete. Too human.

### No Verbal Tics

Real characters have speech patterns. Repeated words, favorite phrases, specific accents or dialects. One character always says "you know?" Another deflects with humor. Another uses formal language. AI characters are interchangeable.

### Too-Perfect Grammar in Casual Speech

Even in dialogue, AI maintains grammatical perfection. No contractions. No dropped g's. No regional speech patterns. It's like everyone was educated at the same prestigious university.

## 5. Known AI Tells — Narrative

Beyond individual elements, AI's approach to *narration itself* is mechanical.

### Summarizing Instead of Dramatizing

AI summarizes when it should dramatize:

*AI:*
> They had a difficult conversation about their future. He expressed his doubts, and she defended her position. Eventually, they reached an understanding.

*Human:*
> "I don't think this is working," he said.
> She set down her cup. "Because I want to move, or because of something else?"
> "Both. Everything."
> She didn't answer for a long time.

### Announcing Emotions

AI announces emotional states rather than demonstrating them:

- "He felt a wave of sadness..."
- "She experienced profound relief..."
- "He was overwhelmed by anger..."

Real writing shows emotion through physical sensation, action, and dialogue. Readers infer the emotion.

### Obvious Foreshadowing

AI's foreshadowing is explicit and clumsy:

> She didn't know it yet, but this decision would change everything.

Or:

> He had no idea that this would be their last conversation.

Real foreshadowing is subtle, even invisible on a first read.

### Wrapping Up Every Scene with a Neat Emotional Bow

AI resolves scenes tidily. A moment of conflict concludes with emotional clarity. A scene ends with a character's realization or decision. Real writing leaves scenes with loose threads, unresolved tension, and ambiguity. Characters leave scenes confused, angry, or uncertain.

## 6. Countermeasures — Vocabulary

### Use Specific, Concrete Words Over Abstract Ones

Replace abstract words with concrete details:

*Abstract (AI-prone):*
> The landscape was beautiful.

*Concrete (human):*
> Dust devils spiraled across the plain. Sage turned gray-green in the heat.

### Regional and Period-Appropriate Vocabulary

If your story is set in 1890s London, use the vocabulary of that time and place. If your character is from rural Kentucky, use regional speech patterns. AI defaults to universal, timeless vocabulary. Human writers ground language in specificity.

### Character-Specific Word Choices

Different characters should have different vocabularies:

- A doctor uses medical terminology.
- A truck driver uses colloquialisms and specific jargon.
- A teenager uses current slang.
- A character with a stutter or speech impediment uses patterns reflecting that.

### Deliberately Imperfect Word Choices

Sometimes choose the slightly wrong word for effect:

> He was dead-set against it. (Not "adamantly opposed")
> She made a mess of the whole thing. (Not "she mishandled the situation")

This creates voice.

### Avoid the Flagged Words Entirely

Go through your work and remove every instance of the 55 flagged words listed above. Replace them with:

- Active verbs
- Concrete nouns
- Specific modifiers
- Words tied to character voice

## 7. Countermeasures — Structure

### Vary Sentence Length Dramatically

Write a 2-word sentence. Then a 50-word sentence. Then a fragment. Create rhythm through variation:

> He stopped. The entire room—every person in it, every object, every shadow—seemed to hold its breath with him. Then someone coughed.

### Use Fragments Intentionally

Fragments aren't errors in fiction. They're tools:

> Gone. The whole thing. Twenty years of work, and it was gone.

### Intentional Run-Ons

Sometimes a run-on sentence captures a character's mental state:

> She thought about leaving, about how easy it would be, about the apartment she'd find in the city, about calling her mother and lying about why she left, about whether he'd even notice or if he'd just fill the space with someone new.

### Asymmetric Paragraphs

Vary paragraph length radically:

> Short paragraph here.

> This is a longer paragraph. It contains multiple sentences. It develops an idea more fully. It creates immersion. The reader settles in. Time slows.

> Quick.

### Break the Rules Strategically

Use passive voice when the agent matters less than the action. Use exclamation marks when a character shouts. Invert word order for emphasis. Break every "rule" you know—but intentionally, for effect.

## 8. Countermeasures — Voice

### Author-Profile-Driven Word Lists

Create a document of:
- **Words you love** (and will use frequently)
- **Words you forbid** (and will never use)
- **Sentence structures you favor** (short and punchy vs. long and lyrical)
- **Metaphor families** you return to

For example, one author might:
- Love: "stripped," "raw," "unraveled," "skin"
- Forbid: "journey," "beacon," "resonate"
- Structure: Favors short sentences and fragments
- Metaphors: Often uses textile/clothing metaphors

### Signature Phrases

Develop phrases unique to your writing:

- A specific oath or curse only your characters use
- A way of describing time ("back when" vs. "in those days" vs. "once")
- Recurring turns of phrase that feel distinctly *yours*

### Recurring Metaphor Families

Don't scatter random metaphors. Build families:

If you favor **nautical metaphors**: anchor, drift, current, undertow, surface, dive, wreckage. They reinforce each other.

If you favor **textile metaphors**: thread, weave, unravel, knit, fray, stitch, seam.

Consistency creates voice.

### Specific Rhythmic Patterns

Train yourself to recognize your natural rhythm. Some writers favor iambic patterns (da-DUM). Others favor spondaic (DUM-DUM). Others are arrhythmic.

Read your work aloud. Feel the rhythm. Let it become your signature.

### Verbal Tics That Feel Human

Identify two or three verbal patterns unique to how *you* write:

- A particular punctuation style (em dashes? Semicolons? Ellipses?)
- A tendency toward specific sentence openings (rarely starting with "He was...")
- A specific way of handling descriptions (minimal vs. lush)
- A particular way characters reveal information (through confession? Through conflict? Through silence?)

These tics, repeated across your work, create the impression of an individual voice. AI lacks this consistency—or has the *wrong kind* of consistency.

## 9. Countermeasures — Content

### Hyper-Specific Details

Replace generic descriptions with specific, irreducible details:

*Generic (AI):*
> The restaurant was nice and had good food.

*Specific (Human):*
> The restaurant had dark wood booths and a jukebox in the corner that still played vinyl. The owner knew everyone's name. She always brought water without being asked and never let your coffee cup go empty.

### Digressive Asides

Real writers digress. They follow tangents. They include information that seems irrelevant but feels true:

> She thought about the time her father took her to the beach, how he'd taught her to body surf, how he'd never learned to swim properly himself.

This doesn't advance the plot. But it makes the character real.

### Characters with Genuinely Ugly Thoughts

AI characters are decent. They have conflicts but remain fundamentally likable. Real characters think terrible things. They resent people they love. They fantasize about cruelty. They have petty, selfish thoughts.

Showing these moments (without judgment) makes characters authentic.

### Moral Ambiguity

AI resolves moral dilemmas. Real fiction leaves them unresolved. A character does something wrong but understandable. A villain has a point. A hero acts selfishly. No character is purely right or wrong.

### Unresolved Tension

Not every scene needs closure. Not every conflict needs resolution. Real fiction ends scenes with questions unanswered, tension unrelieved. The reader (and character) sit with discomfort.

## 10. The Author Profile as Defense

The most powerful defense against AI-generic writing is a *coherent author profile*. This isn't about "finding your voice." It's about creating constraints that make every sentence recognizably *yours*.

### What Is an Author Profile?

An author profile consists of:

1. **Vocabulary constraints** — Words you love, words you forbid
2. **Structural preferences** — Sentence length patterns, paragraph shapes
3. **Narrative habits** — How you handle time, causality, emotion, description
4. **Metaphor families** — The image clusters you return to
5. **Rhythmic signature** — The musicality of your prose
6. **Thematic obsessions** — The ideas and images you can't stop exploring
7. **Character voice markers** — Specific speech patterns tied to character types or backgrounds
8. **Moral perspective** — How you approach judgment and ambiguity

### Why This Defends Against AI

AI generates text by predicting the statistically most likely next word. It has no constraints. When you add constraints—"I never use 'journey'" or "I always use short sentences for emotional moments" or "My characters never apologize"—you create a fingerprint that AI cannot replicate without being given those explicit constraints.

The more specific and idiosyncratic your profile, the more *recognizably human* your writing becomes.

### Building Your Profile

1. **Read your own work.** What patterns do you see?
2. **Identify your favorite words.** Make a list. Use them deliberately.
3. **Identify words you hate.** Ban them. Use Find & Replace if necessary.
4. **Analyze your sentence rhythms.** Are you short and punchy? Long and lyrical? Variable?
5. **Notice your metaphors.** What do you compare things to? What image families repeat?
6. **Study your dialogue.** How do characters differ? What speech patterns repeat?
7. **Document your perspective.** How do you handle morality, emotion, ambiguity?

Then, *commit to the profile*. Don't waiver. Every sentence should be recognizably *you*—not because you're trying to be original, but because you're following your own rules.

---

## Summary: The Human Signal

AI-generated fiction is detectable because it lacks genuine constraints. It optimizes globally for coherence rather than locally for character, voice, and specificity.

Human writing is marked by:

- Wildly variable sentence length
- Specific, irreducible details
- A distinctive vocabulary profile
- Character voices that differ radically
- Structural choices that prioritize effect over elegance
- Moral ambiguity and unresolved tension
- Digression, asides, and seemingly irrelevant details
- A coherent authorial perspective

Your defense is commitment to a personal style—expressed through vocabulary choices, structural patterns, character voices, and narrative perspective that cannot be easily replicated without explicit instruction.

Make your profile idiosyncratic, specific, and unflinching. Every choice should be *yours*—not because it follows a rule, but because it breaks one.
