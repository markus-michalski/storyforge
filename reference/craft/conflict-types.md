# Conflict Types: The Engine of Story

Every story is built on conflict. Not necessarily violence or overt hostility, but the fundamental collision of opposing forces, desires, or values. Conflict is what makes narrative possible. Without it, there's no story—just a sequence of events.

Conflict isn't a problem to solve quickly so you can get to the "real story." Conflict *is* the story. Every meaningful moment in a narrative occurs because of conflict. Your job is to understand the different types of conflict available to you and to deploy them with intention.

The best stories layer multiple types of conflict simultaneously. A character fights an external antagonist (Man vs. Man) while fighting their own fear (Man vs. Self) and against a society that condemns them (Man vs. Society). These conflicts mirror and amplify each other, creating depth and resonance.

## Internal vs. External Conflict: The Mirror Relationship

Internal conflict is the character's struggle with themselves—doubts, fears, competing values, guilt, addiction, identity crisis. It's the psychological landscape.

External conflict is the character's struggle with something outside themselves—an antagonist, a circumstance, a system, nature. It's the physical landscape.

Both must be present. External conflict alone creates a hollow adventure story. Internal conflict alone creates stagnation—a character thinking but never acting, never growing.

The deepest stories have internal and external conflict that *mirror each other*. The external obstacle should force the character to confront their internal obstacle. The character's internal growth should change how they approach the external obstacle.

### The Mirror Structure

A character afraid of commitment (internal conflict) meets someone who demands commitment (external conflict). She must choose: risk her heart or lose the person. The external pressure forces the internal choice. Her decision—to commit or to flee—simultaneously resolves both conflicts.

In The Lord of the Rings, Frodo's internal conflict is his desire for the ring (corruption, obsession) while his external conflict is the journey to destroy it. These aren't separate problems. They're the same problem from different angles. His internal desire mirrors his external enemy. The more obsessed he becomes, the more power the ring has over him. The more power the ring has, the more desperately he wants it.

### Asymmetrical Depth

Sometimes one conflict is deeper than the other, and that's intentional.

In Die Hard, John McClane's external conflict is clear: survive the terrorists and save his wife. His internal conflict is subtler: he's a failed husband and father, emotionally distant, unable to connect. The action thriller plot creates space for character depth. By the end, he's survived externally but transformed internally.

A romance might be the opposite: the internal conflict (learning to trust again after heartbreak) is the primary story, while the external conflict (obstacles to being together) is the machinery that drives it.

## Man vs. Self: The Foundation

This is the most essential conflict type. Every meaningful character must struggle with themselves, regardless of what other conflicts they face.

Self-conflict comes in several varieties:

### Fear and Avoidance

A character is afraid of something—failure, success, vulnerability, change, the unknown. The story is their struggle to face that fear or their flight from it.

In Fleabag, the protagonist avoids genuine connection through humor and deflection. The story is her gradual, painful recognition that avoidance costs her everything and her struggle to connect authentically. The entire series is internal conflict.

### Moral Dilemma

A character faces a choice between competing values, and no choice is clearly right.

In The Remains of the Day, Stevens has served a lord whose politics enabled atrocity. Stevens tells himself he was simply doing his duty. The internal conflict is whether duty absolves him of moral responsibility, or whether his loyalty to a flawed man makes him complicit. He must wrestle with his own guilt and shame.

### Addiction and Compulsion

A character is driven by desires that harm them, and they must choose whether to indulge or resist.

In Requiem for a Dream, all four protagonists are trapped in various addictions. The internal conflict is their struggle against the pull of these addictions, their awareness that the addictions are destroying them, and often their inability to stop despite that awareness.

### Identity Crisis

A character must figure out who they are, often by confronting who they've been taught they should be.

In Middlesex, the protagonist discovers mid-life that they were born intersex but raised female. The entire novel is the internal conflict of reconciling identity, understanding how identity is constructed, and determining who they actually are versus who they've been told to be.

### Competing Desires

A character wants two incompatible things, and must choose which desire to honor.

In Brokeback Mountain, the protagonists want love and also want to preserve their families, their safety, their place in the world. They want to be together and also want to be able to exist in their communities without constant threat. These desires are incompatible, and the tragedy of the story is that no choice fully resolves the internal conflict.

## Man vs. Man: The Antagonist Question

This is conflict with another character—typically the antagonist. But this is where the specificity matters.

The antagonist is not simply the villain. The best antagonists are not evil. They believe they're right. Their values conflict with the protagonist's values, or they're pursuing the same goal and only one can succeed, or they represent something the protagonist must overcome.

### The True Villain

Some antagonists are genuinely villainous—they pursue harm for its own sake or for selfish reasons that don't account for the suffering they cause.

In No Country for Old Men, the killer Anton Chigurh is driven by a philosophy that's internally consistent but sociopathic. He believes in inevitability and determinism and uses violence to express that philosophy. He's not evil in the sense of being motivated by petty selfishness, but he's fundamentally destructive. Facing him tests the protagonist's understanding of fate, chance, and what responsibility means.

### The Rival

A rival is someone pursuing the same goal as the protagonist, and only one can have it. But a rival isn't inherently immoral. They might be genuinely good, just pursuing different priorities.

In a competition, both characters are good. They both deserve to win. The conflict is clean and clear. The antagonism is circumstantial, not moral.

### The Foil

A foil is a character who contrasts with the protagonist, highlighting the protagonist's qualities by being opposite. A foil isn't necessarily an antagonist in the traditional sense—they might not oppose the protagonist's goals. But their mere existence, their different choices and values, creates thematic conflict.

In Crime and Punishment, Raskolnikov and Sonya are thematic foils. They're both dealing with poverty and desperation, but Sonya chooses prostitution and prayer while Raskolnikov chooses murder and philosophy. The conflict is about which response to suffering is more honest, more human. They don't oppose each other's goals directly; they oppose each other's *worldviews*.

### The Mirror Antagonist

A mirror antagonist is someone who is fundamentally similar to the protagonist but who made different choices or values different things. This creates the unsettling recognition that the protagonist could have become this person.

In American Psycho, Patrick Bateman and his colleagues are nearly indistinguishable. The satire is that there's no meaningful difference between them—they're all hollow, performative, violent in their selfishness. The protagonist isn't opposed by someone fundamentally different; he's opposed by people fundamentally identical to him.

### Conflict of Values

The deepest man-vs-man conflicts occur when both parties have legitimate claims.

In 12 Angry Men, the antagonism is between jurors who want to convict and the juror pushing for reasonable doubt. Neither is clearly wrong. Both are trying to serve justice, but they define justice differently and value different kinds of evidence differently. The conflict is real but not melodramatic.

In The Godfather, Michael Corleone and his brother Fredo are in conflict, but not because one is good and one is bad. They both love the family. They simply define loyalty and protection differently. The tragedy is that their love for the same thing drives them apart.

## Man vs. Society: The System as Antagonist

This is the individual against the machine—social systems, cultural norms, power structures, institutions, prejudice.

### Systemic Oppression

A character or group is crushed by systems larger than any individual—racism, classism, sexism, authoritarianism. The antagonist isn't a person; it's a structure.

In The Handmaid's Tale, Offred's conflict is with a theocratic system that has stripped her of autonomy, identity, and choice. The system is the antagonist. Individual characters (the Commander, Nick) are complicated, but the system itself is relentless.

### Conformity Pressure

A society demands the character conform, surrender their individuality, abandon their values. The pressure is often subtle and moral—society doesn't demand explicit obedience; it just makes nonconformity painful.

In Fahrenheit 451, the society doesn't force people to burn books; it makes them *want* to. The conformity pressure is so complete that rebellion feels irrational. The conflict is between the protagonist's growing recognition that something is wrong and the society's comfortable narcosis.

### Injustice

A character recognizes injustice and fights against a system that perpetuates it, knowing the fight might be unwinnable.

In To Kill a Mockingbird, Atticus fights against the racial injustice of the trial system, knowing he'll likely lose. The conflict is between his moral conviction and a system that doesn't value the evidence he presents because the system is corrupted by prejudice.

### Class Conflict

A character is confined by their economic circumstance and must fight against systems designed to keep them trapped.

In Dickens' work, characters are often in conflict with economic systems that benefit from their suffering. The workhouses, the factories, the hierarchies of class are antagonists as much as any person.

## Man vs. Nature: Indifferent Force

Nature as antagonist is powerful because nature doesn't care. It's not malicious. It's just indifferent to human survival and desire.

### Survival Against Environment

A character must survive against natural forces—weather, terrain, animals, disease.

In Jack London's stories, the frozen north is the antagonist. It's not evil, but it's implacable. The character's survival depends on understanding nature and respecting its power. Many of London's protagonists lose because they underestimate nature's indifference.

### Disaster Narratives

Natural disasters force characters to confront their fragility and mortality.

In volcanic eruption narratives, earthquake narratives, and tsunami narratives, the antagonist is the force of nature itself. The conflict is survival, yes, but also the philosophical question that arises: What does it mean that nature can unmake everything we've built? What do we value when everything else is stripped away?

### The Lifespan Antagonist

Time and mortality are nature's conflict. A character ages, weakens, declines toward death.

In The Remains of the Day, Stevens is in conflict with his own aging. He's spent his life in service, and now his life is nearly over. He realizes he's wasted decades, and he cannot get them back. Nature (time) is the antagonist he cannot fight.

### Biological Imperative

A character is driven by biological need—hunger, reproduction, disease, pain. These drives come from nature and often conflict with the character's conscious choices.

In many survival narratives, the biological drive to eat conflicts with the moral choice not to eat something forbidden (human flesh, sacred animals). The conflict is between the body's demands and the mind's values.

## Man vs. Technology: Modern and Timeless

As technology becomes more central to human life, this conflict type grows in importance.

### Artificial Intelligence and Autonomy

A character must confront AI that's becoming more sophisticated, more autonomous, more unknowable.

In Blade Runner, the conflict is whether androids deserve personhood and autonomy if they can pass the Turing test. The "man vs. technology" conflict is really a question about what counts as alive, what counts as human.

### Dehumanization Through Tools

Technology designed to improve life instead diminishes human connection and agency.

In Wall-E, humanity has become so dependent on technology that they've surrendered agency and physical capability. The conflict is between the comfortable slavery of technological dependence and the struggle of genuine autonomy.

### Surveillance and Control

Technology designed for convenience becomes a tool of control and loss of privacy.

In 1984, Big Brother uses technology to surveil and control. The conflict is between the individual's desire for autonomy and the system's desire for total control through technological omniscience.

### The Uncanny

Technology that mimics humanity without being humanity creates existential unease.

In some science fiction, the conflict isn't about AI taking over but about the uncanny valley—the discomfort of something almost human but not quite. A character must confront what happens when the distinction between human and artificial blurs.

## Man vs. Fate/God: Free Will and Destiny

This is the conflict between determinism and choice, between what's predetermined and what can be changed.

### Prophecy and Destiny

A character is told what will happen (prophecy) and must choose how to respond. Often, the attempt to avoid fate leads to fate's fulfillment.

In Greek tragedy, Oedipus hears a prophecy that he'll kill his father and marry his mother. He runs from his adoptive family to avoid the prophecy. In running, he fulfills it. The conflict is between his will to avoid a terrible destiny and a cosmos that seems determined to make it happen.

### Divine Will vs. Individual Will

A character is commanded by God (or gods, or a religious system claiming divine authority) to do something. The conflict is between following that command and following their own moral compass.

In The Brothers Karamazov, Ivan rebels against God because he cannot accept a God who allows children to suffer. The conflict is between submitting to divine will and exercising moral judgment. Dostoevsky doesn't resolve it; he makes the reader wrestle with both sides.

### Fate and Responsibility

If events are fated, is the character responsible for their actions? This is a deep philosophical conflict that manifests as a character's struggle with agency.

In No Country for Old Men, Chigurh believes in determinism. Others believe in chance and choice. The conflict between these worldviews shapes how characters interpret what happens to them. Did the protagonist fail because of fate, or because of choices he made?

### The Limits of Agency

A character discovers that despite their will and effort, certain outcomes are beyond their control.

In many tragedy narratives, the character struggles against inevitable decline (age, disease, death). The conflict isn't with a person or even a system, but with the fundamental conditions of existence.

## Conflict Escalation: The Try-Fail Cycle

Static conflict is boring. Conflict must escalate—each obstacle must be harder than the last, each setback must raise the stakes, each attempt to resolve the conflict must fail in a way that deepens it.

### Complications vs. Setbacks

A complication is an additional obstacle. "The bridge is out, so we have to find another way."

A setback is an obstacle that also costs the character something. "The bridge is out, and we've already lost half our supplies trying to cross it."

Setbacks are more powerful because they raise stakes. Each failure doesn't just delay the character; it damages their resources, their resolve, their relationships.

### The Try-Fail Cycle

The character tries to solve the problem. They fail. This failure teaches them something, and they try a different approach. They fail again, differently. Over time, they gain knowledge, resources, allies, and understanding.

The try-fail cycle is not the character failing over and over identically. Each failure must move the story forward, teach the character something new, and raise the stakes.

### Raising Stakes Without Melodrama

A common mistake is thinking stakes are raised by adding violence or physical danger. That works sometimes, but it's blunt.

Subtler stakes are often more powerful: a relationship at risk, reputation damaged, trust lost, an opportunity that won't come again, time running out.

In The Remains of the Day, the stakes escalate not through action or danger, but through the protagonist's growing realization of what he's given up. As he reviews his life, he sees more clearly what his loyalty has cost him. The stakes are emotional, not physical, but they're devastating.

### Momentum and Pacing

Escalation is also about pacing. Scenes of tension followed by moments of relative calm. But the calm should be shorter and the tension longer as the story progresses. The ratio of tension to calm shifts.

Early in the story: Long calm scene, brief tension, long calm scene.
Late in the story: Brief calm, long tension, brief calm, long tension.

This change in pacing itself creates the sense of escalation.

## Conflict on Every Level: No Empty Scenes

A scene without conflict is a dead scene. It might be beautiful, it might reveal character, but if there's no tension or opposition, the reader's attention drops.

Conflict doesn't mean violence or overt antagonism. It means something is at stake in the scene, something is being decided, something is in question.

### Scene-Level Conflict

Every scene should have conflict. Not necessarily man-vs-man, but tension of some kind:
- A character wants something and is denied it (blocked desire)
- A character must choose between two things they want (competing desires)
- A character's values are being tested (moral conflict)
- A character learns something that changes their understanding (informational conflict)
- Two characters disagree about something (ideological conflict)

Even a quiet scene of two people having coffee can have conflict if there's subtext—if one character is hiding something, or if there's tension between what they say and what they mean, or if they're speaking around a dangerous topic.

### Chapter-Level Conflict

A chapter should complicate the character's situation or deepen the central conflict. If a chapter ends in the same place it began, with nothing changed or learned, it's slack.

Each chapter should escalate or deepen something. A revelation that changes the character's understanding. A setback that costs the character something. A choice that closes off options and narrows the path forward.

### Book-Level Conflict

The entire narrative is built around a central conflict or question. Everything that happens is related to that central tension. Subplots mirror it, themes elaborate it, scenes illustrate it.

### The Discipline of Checking Conflict

In revision, read through your manuscript and note the conflict in every scene:
- What's at stake?
- What does the character want in this scene?
- What's blocking them?
- What's the emotional temperature?

If you find scenes with no conflict, rewrite them to introduce tension, or cut them. There's rarely a good reason to keep a scene that has no dramatic purpose.

## Moral Dilemmas: No Right Answer

The deepest conflicts have no "right" answer. The character must choose, and any choice costs something.

### The Trolley Problem in Fiction

The classical trolley problem: You can pull a lever and divert a train from killing five people to killing one person. Do you pull the lever?

In fiction, moral dilemmas create conflict because the character is forced to act, and whatever they do is ethically compromised. The reader doesn't know what they would do, and that uncertainty creates tension.

### Sophie's Choice Structure

In Sophie's Choice, Sophie must choose which of her children will be killed. She must choose, and the choice defines her morally and psychologically. There is no good outcome. The conflict is that she must act despite having no good options.

### Trolley Problem Variations

Make the dilemma specific and personal. Not "Should you sacrifice yourself for the group?" but "Should you sacrifice your child for five strangers?" Not "Is lying justified?" but "Is lying justified if the truth will destroy the person you love?"

The more specific and personal the dilemma, the more the reader feels the character's anguish.

### Discomfort as Purpose

Moral dilemmas are supposed to make the reader uncomfortable. They're supposed to make the reader question their own values. If you're writing a moral dilemma and the reader feels comfortable, the dilemma isn't working.

The discomfort is the point. It forces engagement. It forces the reader to ask: "What would I do?" And if the character chooses differently than the reader would, the reader is forced to wrestle with why—is the character immoral, or do they see something the reader is missing?

### Avoiding False Balance

Don't create a moral dilemma where one choice is clearly better and the character agonizes over nothing. "Should I save my family or my country?" only works if both are genuinely important to the character, and saving both is genuinely impossible.

## Layering Conflicts: Depth Through Simultaneity

The best stories don't have one conflict. They have multiple conflicts at different scales, operating simultaneously, mirroring each other, amplifying each other.

### Internal and External Mirror

A character's internal conflict (fear of commitment) mirrors their external conflict (a relationship demanding commitment). As they struggle with the external obstacle, they're forced to confront the internal one.

### Multiple Antagonists at Different Levels

A character is fighting an external antagonist (a person) while fighting an internal antagonist (their own fear or shame) while fighting a social antagonist (a system that disadvantages them).

The Godfather has Michael in conflict with his brother Fredo (man vs. man), with his desire to be legitimate (man vs. self), and with the system of organized crime he's trapped in (man vs. society). These conflicts intersect and amplify each other.

### Subplot Conflicts

Subplots should have conflicts that mirror or contrast with the main plot. This deepens the exploration of the central thematic question without being repetitive.

### Escalating Conflict Scales

Early conflicts might be small and personal. As the story progresses, conflicts expand in scale. A conflict with one person becomes a conflict with a community becomes a conflict with a system.

Or the opposite: A character facing a large external conflict slowly discovers an internal conflict underneath, which becomes the primary story.

## Conflict and Character: Revealing Through Opposition

Conflict is the primary way characters reveal themselves. A character's values, priorities, and psychology are revealed through what they choose when under pressure.

In calm moments, people are curated versions of themselves. In conflict, they're raw and honest. A character choosing to sacrifice for someone else is a choice that reveals values. A character choosing to betray is equally revealing.

The protagonist's conflict is the stage for their growth. They begin with certain beliefs, values, fears. The conflict tests these. The character changes or dies (literally or morally). That transformation *is* the character arc, and the character arc is driven by conflict.

Write your conflicts with intention. Understand what each conflict reveals about the character. Understand how each conflict mirrors other conflicts in the narrative. Understand how escalation serves both plot and character.

Conflict isn't an obstacle to the story. Conflict is the story. Master it, and everything else falls into place.
