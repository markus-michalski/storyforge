# Fiction Writing: 50 Essential Do's and Don'ts

This guide distills the core principles of narrative craft into actionable rules. These aren't laws—they're tools. Each can be broken. But break them intentionally, understanding what you're sacrificing.

---

## DO's (25 Rules for Better Fiction)

### DO 1: Start Late, Leave Early

Every scene should begin as close to its turning point as possible. Don't show the character waking up, getting dressed, driving to the location. Start at the moment something changes.

**Why:** Respects the reader's time. Maintains tension. Gets to the point.

**Example:**
> NOT: "Sarah woke up, showered, and drove to the coffee shop where she would meet James."
> YES: "Sarah spotted James through the window. He was already there, coffee in hand, looking at his watch."

### DO 2: Every Scene Needs Conflict

Conflict doesn't mean violence or shouting. It means *something at stake*. A character wants X. Something prevents them. They must choose or act.

**Why:** Without conflict, a scene is a description. With it, a scene is a story.

**Example:** A conversation between two friends isn't inherently dramatic. But if one friend wants to confess something and fears the other's judgment, the scene has tension.

### DO 3: Show Characters Through Action

Don't describe a character's courage. Show them walking into danger. Don't explain their kindness. Show them helping when it costs them something.

**Why:** Action reveals character more reliably than description. "She was brave" tells. "She stepped forward when everyone else retreated" shows.

**Example:**
> NOT: "Tom was a generous person who always helped others."
> YES: "Tom gave his coat to the homeless man, even though it was the only warm thing he owned."

### DO 4: Use Active Verbs, Concrete Nouns

Active verbs move. Concrete nouns ground. Together, they create sentences readers can see.

**Why:** They're precise. They're visual. They're efficient.

**Example:**
> NOT: "The waves were crashing loudly against the shore."
> YES: "Waves crashed against the shore."

Or better yet: "The waves hammered the rocks."

### DO 5: Write Dialogue With Subtext

The best dialogue doesn't say what characters mean. It circles around it. Characters speak to hide, to wound, to deflect—not always to communicate directly.

**Why:** Real people don't say what they mean. Subtext creates tension and realism.

**Example:**
> NOT: "I'm angry at you because you lied to me."
> YES: "So, did you have a nice lunch? With anyone special?"

(The unspoken accusation is louder than the direct statement.)

### DO 6: Engage All Five Senses

Describe what characters see, hear, smell, taste, touch. Don't default to visual. A character's emotional state is often best conveyed through physical sensation.

**Why:** Sensory details immerse the reader. They ground abstract emotion in the body.

**Example:**
> NOT: "She was nervous."
> YES: "Her throat was dry. The coffee tasted like metal. She'd bitten her lip again."

### DO 7: Give Every Chapter Its Own Mini-Arc

Each chapter should have a beginning, middle, and end. A setup, a complication, and a resolution (or revelation). Even within a larger story, chapters should feel complete.

**Why:** Readers experience satisfaction on multiple levels. It also makes chapters gripping—readers want to finish the chapter to see how it resolves.

### DO 8: Plant and Payoff (Chekhov's Gun)

If you mention a detail, it must matter later. If a character mentions they're afraid of water, and they later cross a river without comment, you've broken a promise to the reader.

**Why:** Readers pay attention. When they notice a detail, they expect it to matter. Breaking that expectation feels like cheating.

**Example:** A character has a fear of fire. Later, they must walk through a burning building. That detail you planted in Act One pays off in Act Three.

### DO 9: Differentiate Character Voices

Each character should have a distinct way of speaking. Vocabulary, sentence structure, speech patterns should differ.

**Why:** It makes characters memorable and realistic. Readers should know who's speaking without attribution.

**Example:**
> Character A: "I'm upset. I feel betrayed."
> Character B: "You messed up, and I ain't about to pretend I ain't mad."
> Character C: "One is compelled to acknowledge a certain disappointment in your behavior."

Different vocabularies. Different rhythms. Different people.

### DO 10: Use Specific Details Over Generic

Specific details make scenes vivid. "The coffee shop" is forgettable. "The coffee shop with the chipped turquoise mugs and the owner who wrote poetry on the blackboard" is memorable.

**Why:** Specificity is the currency of authenticity. It's also how readers believe in your world.

### DO 11: Read Your Dialogue Aloud

Dialogue should sound like speech, not how people write. Hearing it helps you catch rhythm, naturalness, and authenticity.

**Why:** Your ear is smarter than your eyes. Awkward dialogue jumps out when spoken.

### DO 12: Make Your Protagonist Act, Not React

Your character should drive the story. They should make choices, take actions, pursue goals. They shouldn't just respond to what happens to them.

**Why:** An active protagonist is compelling. A reactive one is passive. Readers follow people who *do things*.

### DO 13: Keep Backstory in the Background

Don't dump backstory in exposition. Weave it into dialogue, action, and revelation. Let readers discover the past gradually.

**Why:** Exposition stops momentum. Discovering backstory through action maintains it.

**Example:**
> NOT: "Five years ago, Sarah's father had died in a car accident, which made her afraid of driving."
> YES: Sarah's hands gripped the steering wheel. She was doing 35 in a 55-zone. The driver behind her flashed their lights.

### DO 14: End Chapters With a Hook

Each chapter should end with a question, revelation, or complication that makes the reader want the next chapter.

**Why:** Hooks maintain momentum. They're the narrative equivalent of a cliffhanger.

**Example:** End a chapter with a doorbell ringing at midnight. Or a character's realization. Or a question that hasn't been answered.

### DO 15: Vary Sentence Length and Structure

Mix short sentences with long. Use fragments. Use complex constructions. Avoid uniformity.

**Why:** Variation creates rhythm and emphasis. It keeps prose from feeling mechanical.

### DO 16: Use White Space for Emphasis

A single line. A paragraph with one sentence. A scene break. White space creates pause and emphasis.

**Why:** Silence is as powerful as speech. Pause is as powerful as action.

**Example:**
> The gun was on the table.
>
> He looked at it for ten minutes.

(The white space creates tension.)

### DO 17: Make Your Antagonist Believe They're Right

The best antagonists don't think they're villains. They have their own logic, their own moral framework. They believe their cause is just.

**Why:** It makes conflict more interesting. It prevents caricature. It forces your protagonist to actually *argue* rather than simply oppose evil.

### DO 18: Give Characters Wants AND Needs (Different)

A character's *want* is what they consciously desire. Their *need* is what they actually require for growth or fulfillment. Often, these conflict.

**Why:** This creates the internal conflict that drives character arcs.

**Example:** A character wants fame (want). They need authentic connection (need). The story could be about pursuing one and discovering the other's importance.

### DO 19: Let Silence Speak in Dialogue

Sometimes, the most powerful dialogue beat is what's *not* said. A character doesn't respond. A conversation trails off. Silence carries meaning.

**Why:** It mimics real human interaction. It allows tension to build.

**Example:**
> "I never stopped loving you," he said.
> She didn't answer.

(Her silence says what words can't.)

### DO 20: Trust Your Reader's Intelligence

Don't explain everything. Don't resolve all ambiguities. Don't tell readers what to think. Give them information and let them fill the gaps.

**Why:** Readers are smart. Trusting them feels respectful. It also invites them to participate in meaning-making.

### DO 21: Revise With Fresh Eyes

Don't revise the day you finish. Let it sit. Days or weeks later, you'll see it differently. You'll notice what doesn't work. You'll see where you're being unclear.

**Why:** Proximity blinds you. Distance clarifies.

### DO 22: Cut Ruthlessly — If in Doubt, Cut

If you're unsure whether a paragraph, scene, or chapter is necessary, it probably isn't. Cut it. If it's essential, you'll miss it and know to restore it.

**Why:** Every word should serve the story. Uncertain words are usually dead weight.

### DO 23: Ground Abstract Emotion in Physical Sensation

Don't write "He felt sad." Write: "His chest was tight. He couldn't swallow. When he tried to stand, his legs didn't cooperate."

**Why:** Emotion becomes real through the body. Readers experience emotion vicariously through physical sensation.

### DO 24: Make Every Word Earn Its Place

Read each sentence. Does every word matter? Does every word change meaning if removed? If a word is decorative or predictable, cut it.

**Why:** Efficiency. Precision. Impact.

### DO 25: Write the Scene That Scares You

The scene you're avoiding—the one that feels too intimate, too risky, too honest—is often the best. Write it. It's where authenticity lives.

**Why:** Vulnerability in writing resonates. The scenes you're scared to write are often the ones readers feel most deeply.

## DON'Ts (25 Rules to Avoid)

### DON'T 1: Open With Weather or Waking Up

These are passive beginnings. They put the character in a static situation. The reader learns about the world but not about conflict or change.

**Why:** They're clichés. They don't establish stakes.

**Example to avoid:** "The sun rose over the city. Sarah woke up and looked out the window at the gray sky."

**Better:** "Sarah's alarm went off at 5:47—thirteen minutes before she needed it to. She turned it off and didn't go back to sleep."

### DON'T 2: Info-Dump Backstory in Chapter 1

Readers don't need to know everything about a character's history immediately. Dole out backstory throughout the narrative.

**Why:** It stops momentum. It feels like exposition. It boring.

**Example to avoid:** "Marcus had grown up in Detroit. His father was a mechanic. His mother left when he was five. He had one sister, who he barely spoke to anymore..."

**Better:** Let readers discover Marcus's backstory through his reactions, choices, and gradually revealed details.

### DON'T 3: Use Adverbs in Dialogue Tags

"He said angrily." "She whispered softly." These are lazy. The dialogue should convey tone without the adverb.

**Why:** Adverbs tell readers how to interpret. Let the dialogue speak for itself.

**Example to avoid:** "I'm leaving," she said sadly.

**Better:** "I'm leaving." She looked at the door, not at him.

### DON'T 4: Have Characters Explain Things They Both Know

If two characters both know information, don't have them discuss it for the reader's benefit. This is lazy exposition.

**Why:** It feels unnatural. Real people don't explain things they both understand.

**Example to avoid:**
> "As you know, we've been friends since high school."
> "Yes, and you introduced me to my husband."

**Better:** Assume readers will catch on. If you must reference shared history, do it naturally.

### DON'T 5: Write "She Felt Sad"

Show sadness through action, gesture, dialogue. Don't name the emotion and expect the reader to experience it.

**Why:** Naming emotions tells. Showing emotions immerses.

**Example to avoid:** "He felt angry and betrayed."

**Better:** "He crumpled the letter. His hands shook. When she walked in, he didn't look at her."

### DON'T 6: Resolve Conflict Too Easily

If a character faces a real problem, it should be genuinely hard to solve. Easy resolutions feel cheap.

**Why:** Readers value stakes. Easy victories diminish them.

**Example to avoid:** A character is terrified of flying. On page 20, they fly. On page 21, they realize it wasn't so bad.

**Better:** They battle their fear throughout the narrative. Maybe they fly and it's still terrifying. Maybe they don't fly. The fear doesn't evaporate.

### DON'T 7: Make All Characters Sound the Same

Variation in dialogue is essential. Each character should have a distinct voice.

**Why:** Readers need to distinguish characters. Sameness is boring and confusing.

### DON'T 8: Use Cliché Metaphors

"Brave as a lion." "Heart of gold." "Love is a journey." These have been used so often they're invisible.

**Why:** Clichés are dead. They don't create images. They feel lazy.

**Example to avoid:** "Her smile was like sunshine."

**Better:** Find a metaphor specific to your story. "Her smile was like a door opening in a dark room."

### DON'T 9: Over-Describe Physical Appearances

A few specific details matter. Too many and the character becomes a catalog.

**Why:** Readers fill in details themselves. Too much description stops the narrative and makes characters feel mannequin-like.

**Example to avoid:** "She had blonde hair, blue eyes, a small nose, full lips, pale skin, long legs, and wore a red dress."

**Better:** "She had a scar on her left eyebrow and painted her nails black."

### DON'T 10: Have the Hero Saved by Coincidence

If the protagonist is in genuine danger and they're saved by luck or coincidence, you've undermined the conflict.

**Why:** It feels like cheating. Readers accept that stakes matter. Luck undercuts that.

**Example to avoid:** The villain has the hero trapped. Suddenly, a convenient earthquake or stranger arrives and saves them.

**Better:** The hero saves themselves through cleverness, courage, or sacrifice. Or they don't get saved.

### DON'T 11: Skip the Emotional Aftermath of Big Events

If something major happens—a death, a betrayal, a revelation—show the character's reaction. Don't jump immediately to the next plot point.

**Why:** Emotion and consequence matter. Skipping the aftermath feels cold and unreal.

**Example to avoid:** A character's parent dies on page 50. By page 51, the character is calmly pursuing their goals.

**Better:** Show them struggling. Show them angry, disoriented, desperate, numb. Show how the death *changes* them.

### DON'T 12: Repeat the Same Information

If you've told readers something, you don't need to tell them again in the next chapter. Trust that they remember.

**Why:** Repetition feels didactic and patronizing. It slows pacing.

### DON'T 13: Use Filter Words

"He saw..." "She heard..." "He thought..." These create distance between reader and character. Cut them.

**Why:** They add a layer of narration you don't need. Without them, the reader experiences events directly.

**Example:**
> NOT: "He heard the door slam."
> YES: "The door slammed."

### DON'T 14: Write Filler Scenes

Every scene should advance character, plot, or theme. If a scene doesn't do any of these, delete it.

**Why:** Filler feels like padding. Readers notice and resent it.

**Test:** Could you delete this scene without losing anything important? If yes, delete it.

### DON'T 15: Telegraph Your Twists

If a twist is coming, don't hint at it obviously. Readers will guess. The reveal should be surprising, not expected.

**Why:** Telegraphing undermines the impact of revelation.

**Example to avoid:** Establishing that a character has a secret and hinting repeatedly that it's dark. When it's revealed, readers have already guessed.

**Better:** Plant clues readers won't notice until hindsight. Misdirect them. The reveal should feel inevitable only after it happens.

### DON'T 16: Be Afraid of Short Chapters

A chapter can be two pages. It can be two paragraphs. Short chapters create pacing and momentum.

**Why:** There's no law about chapter length. Use the length that serves your story.

### DON'T 17: Over-Explain Jokes or Emotions

If a joke lands, don't explain it. If a character's emotion is clear, don't elaborate. Trust the reader.

**Why:** Explanation kills the effect. It treats readers like they're stupid.

**Example to avoid:**
> "I'd offer to help, but I'm useless with maps," he said, which was funny because he was actually very good with maps.

**Better:** "I'd offer to help, but I'm useless with maps." (Let readers get the joke themselves.)

### DON'T 18: Head-Hop (Switch POV Mid-Scene)

If you're in a character's POV, stay there for the scene. Don't jump into another character's head.

**Why:** It's disorienting. It breaks the reader's identification with the character whose POV you established.

**Note:** This rule can be bent in omniscient narration, but even then, clarity matters.

### DON'T 19: Rely on Exclamation Marks for Emphasis

Exclamation marks are tools, not solutions. Too many feel juvenile and weaken impact.

**Why:** Proper punctuation and word choice create emphasis more effectively than exclamation marks.

**Example:**
> NOT: "I can't believe this! This is insane!"
> YES: "I can't believe this." (The simple statement is stronger.)

### DON'T 20: Write Perfect Characters

Characters with no flaws are boring. Even heroes should have genuine weaknesses, blind spots, or selfish impulses.

**Why:** Perfection isn't human. Flaws make characters relatable and interesting.

### DON'T 21: Use Passive Voice in Action Scenes

Passive voice weakens action. Use active verbs and vivid subjects.

**Why:** Active voice has momentum. Passive voice drags.

**Example:**
> NOT: "The gun was fired and the window was shattered."
> YES: "He fired. The window exploded."

### DON'T 22: Forget the Passage of Time

Make it clear how much time passes between scenes. If a week has gone by, readers should know.

**Why:** Without time markers, scenes feel like they're happening in a vacuum. Readers lose orientation.

### DON'T 23: Make World-Building a Lecture

Readers don't want a lesson. Weave world details into narrative. Let them discover the world through story.

**Why:** Exposition stops story. Integration maintains it.

**Example to avoid:** A character explaining the history and rules of a magical system to another character (who would already know).

**Better:** Let world details emerge through action, dialogue, and implication.

### DON'T 24: Sacrifice Story for Style (or Vice Versa)

A beautiful sentence that doesn't move the story forward is decoration. A plot that ignores character and voice is mechanical. Both matter.

**Why:** Story and style reinforce each other. Either without the other is incomplete.

### DON'T 25: Forget This: All Rules Can Be Broken — Intentionally

Every rule above can be broken. Deliberately. For effect. If you understand why a rule exists, you can break it and know what you're sacrificing.

**Why:** Rules are tools, not laws. Master them, then use them strategically.

**Example:** You usually vary sentence length. But for one sentence, you write five short sentences in a row. The violation of your established pattern creates emphasis.

---

## How to Use These Rules

1. **Learn them.** Understand why each rule exists.

2. **Apply them.** Write following the rules. Notice how they improve your work.

3. **Recognize violations.** As you read your work, notice where you break rules.

4. **Evaluate violations.** Did you break the rule intentionally? Does it serve your story? Or is it a mistake?

5. **Keep what works.** Some rules will feel natural to you. Embrace them.

6. **Question what doesn't.** Some rules will feel restrictive. That's fine—but understand what you're gaining by breaking them.

7. **Develop your own rules.** Eventually, you'll create personal rules that define your voice.

---

## The Real Rule

All of this can be summarized in one principle: **Serve the story.**

Every choice—vocabulary, structure, detail, dialogue, pacing—should serve what the story is trying to do. If a rule helps you serve the story, use it. If breaking a rule serves the story, break it.

The best writers know the rules so thoroughly that they can break them effectively. They understand what each rule protects against and what they're risking by ignoring it.

Your job is to understand these rules, practice them, and then use them—or break them—in service of something worth reading.
