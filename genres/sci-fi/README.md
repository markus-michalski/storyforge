# Science Fiction

## Overview

Science fiction explores the consequences of speculative technology, scientific discovery, or alternate physical laws, using the "what if" to examine human behavior, society, and philosophy. Unlike fantasy, SF is grounded in internal logical consistency with science as we understand it (or a plausible extrapolation). The genre's power lies not in the technology itself but in how it reshapes human experience, relationships, and moral frameworks.

## Characteristics

| Element | Convention |
|---------|-----------|
| Tone | Can range from hard problem-solving to philosophical inquiry to character-driven vulnerability |
| Pacing | Varies: hard SF often slower for explanation; space opera faster; literary SF contemplative |
| POV | Any POV works; SF often uses multiple perspectives to explore ideological conflicts |
| Typical Length | 80,000-120,000 words depending on complexity and scope |
| Chapter Length | 2,500-4,500 words; balance technical explanation with narrative momentum |
| Structure | Problem-solution (inciting incident is a technological crisis), exploration-discovery (protagonist encounters new world/tech), or character-driven transformation |

## Key Conventions

- **The science or technology has real consequences.** If you introduce faster-than-light travel, time dilation, or AI consciousness, those elements fundamentally reshape society and plot.
- **Speculate thoughtfully.** A "what if" should logically follow from its premise. Readers may forgive soft science but not logical leaps within your established rules.
- **The future/alternate world reflects human nature.** Technology changes tools, not morality. Power structures, greed, love, and fear persist. Show how humans adapt their vices to new tools.
- **Exposition should feel natural.** A character unfamiliar with the tech can ask questions; a character fluent in it should explain via dialogue and action, not infodumps.
- **Hard constraints breed creativity.** Limitations on AI behavior, fuel, bandwidth, or mutation push characters toward ingenuity. Unlimited power is boring.
- **Social SF is as valid as technological SF.** Speculating about economic systems, reproduction, or consciousness is SF just as much as space travel.
- **Ground the extraordinary in the mundane.** A character eating breakfast on a space station, handling bureaucracy on a colonized planet, or struggling with AI relationships—these details sell the world.

## Common Tropes (Use Wisely)

- **Humanity's last hope** – Effective when the stakes are genuinely existential; clichéd when it's Earth vs. Invaders #47.
- **The rogue AI** – Fresh if the AI has alien goals or consciousness; tired if it's just "evil computer wants to kill humans."
- **The dystopian government** – Works when the oppression is specific and the resistance is cost-heavy; fails when it's a generic "they're bad" setup.
- **Time travel and paradoxes** – Engaging if the central conflict hinges on temporal mechanics; exhausting if time travel is a plot-hole escape hatch.
- **First contact with aliens** – Excellent hook if the aliens are truly alien; disappointing if they're humans in rubber masks with one shared trait.
- **Hacker saves the day** – Works if hacking is a character skill shown repeatedly; hollow if the protagonist suddenly knows code in chapter 20.
- **The android/cyborg becoming human** – Powerful when it questions what "human" means; clichéd if the answer is "emotions and free will" (see: every second SF novel).

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)

- **Exposition over narrative.** Explaining your world's technology is not a plot. Show it in action; readers learn faster from seeing consequences than from lectures.
- **Inconsistent technology rules.** If your AI can't lie, it can't lie. If FTL travel has fuel costs, enforce those costs. Violating your own rules for plot convenience is visible and jarring.
- **Future slang for its own sake.** A few neologisms add flavor; a glossary's worth of made-up words taxes reading comprehension and feels like padding.
- **Assuming the reader understands the science.** Even hard SF must explain its central mechanism clearly enough for engagement. If readers are lost, they stop caring.
- **Telling instead of showing implications.** Don't have characters discuss how climate change affects them; show them rationing water, migrating, adjusting daily life.
- **Forgetting that future people are still people.** They have the same emotional needs, flaws, and desires as us. Don't make them robots just because they're futuristic.
- **Ignoring unintended consequences.** If you introduce a technology that solves a major problem, show how society adapts and what new problems emerge.
- **Hard sci-fi pacing without characterization.** Even the most technical SF needs emotional stakes and character development. Dry problem-solving is for technical papers.

## Recommended Story Structures

- **The Problem-Solution Arc** – Inciting incident introduces the challenge (plague, invasion, technological discovery), rising action attempts solutions, climax resolves or reframes the problem.
- **Exploration-Discovery** – Protagonist enters new environment/encounters new technology, learns its rules through trial, must master or overcome it.
- **The Character Journey through Systemic Change** – Protagonist witnesses/experiences transformation of society due to technology; story charts their adaptation and moral position.
- **Nested Mysteries** – Each solved mystery reveals a deeper layer of truth about the technology or world. Philosophical and plot-driven in equal measure.

## Subgenres

| Subgenre | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Hard SF | Emphasizes scientific accuracy and logical consistency. Technology is central. Often slower-paced. Readers expect accurate physics, biology, or engineering. |
| Soft SF | Prioritizes social sciences, psychology, or philosophy over hard science. "What if" is psychological or sociological rather than technological. |
| Space Opera | Large-scale, multi-world or multi-system SF with epic scope. Adventure and spectacle are as important as ideology. Often includes magical-feeling tech. |
| Cyberpunk | Near-future urban setting with advanced tech, AI, virtual reality, and corporate dominance. Gritty, morally gray, fast-paced. Emphasis on hacking and information as power. |
| Dystopian | Future society is oppressive or degraded. Emphasis on societal critique. Often climaxes with revolution or acceptance of a terrible truth. |
| Post-Apocalyptic | Civilization has collapsed. Survivors navigate scarcity and rebuild or accept a diminished world. Often combines with other genres (post-apocalyptic SF, post-apocalyptic fantasy). |
| Time Travel | Central to plot. Can explore paradoxes, alternate timelines, or causality. Risk of becoming convoluted; needs clear internal logic. |

| Author | Notable Work | Known For |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Ted Chiang | Stories of Your Life and Others | Philosophical speculation; linguistic and scientific rigor; emotional core grounded in high concepts |
| N.K. Jemisin | The Broken Earth trilogy | SF with fantasy elements; social commentary on power and oppression; devastating worldbuilding |
| Andy Weir | The Martian | Hard SF problem-solving; humor balancing tension; accessible scientific detail; protagonist ingenuity |
| Mary Robinette Kowal | The Calculating Stars | Near-future hard SF; alternate history; space exploration; character-driven alongside technical excellence |

## Genre-Mixing Notes

- **SF + Fantasy:** Tricky. Both demand internal logic but may have different rule-sets. Works when clearly delineated (SF in some areas, magic in others) or when SF explains the fantastical (Clarke's law: sufficiently advanced tech is indistinguishable from magic).
- **SF + Horror:** Excellent pairing. Alien horror, body horror, AI horror, or existential dread work very well in SF settings.
- **SF + Thriller:** Works excellently. High-stakes tech problems or space catastrophes create natural tension. Pacing accelerates toward climax.
- **SF + Romance:** Viable but requires balancing speculative interest with emotional stakes. Don't let worldbuilding overshadow character connection.
- **SF + Mystery:** Great combination. SF mysteries often revolve around understanding the technology or discovering hidden truths about the world.
- **SF + Literary Fiction:** Possible with emphasis on prose quality and thematic depth. Hard SF can feel dry; literary SF deepens philosophical inquiry.
