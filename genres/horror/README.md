# Horror

## Overview

Horror fiction exploits the reader's primal fears and anxieties, creating sustained psychological unease or visceral terror. The genre hinges on the reader's belief in a threat—whether supernatural, psychological, or physical—and the character's (and reader's) inability to escape or fully understand that threat. Effective horror operates at the intersection of empathy and dread: we care deeply about the characters precisely because their suffering becomes our own.

## Characteristics

| Element | Convention |
|---------|-----------|
| Tone | Dread, unease, tension, building toward terror or cathartic release |
| Pacing | Slow-burn atmosphere with sudden jolts; peaks and valleys of tension |
| POV | First-person (intimate terror) or close third (controlled focalisation of dread) |
| Typical Length | 70,000-100,000 words (room for atmosphere and buildup) |
| Chapter Length | 2,000-4,000 words; shorter chapters accelerate toward climax |
| Structure | Three-act structure with inciting incident (the threat arrives), escalation (understanding the threat), and confrontation/survival |

## Key Conventions

- **The threat is credible.** Horror readers suspend disbelief readily, but the rules of the threat must be internally consistent. If ghosts operate by certain logic, breaking those rules feels like cheating.
- **Isolation amplifies dread.** Whether literal (haunted house, remote location) or psychological (mistrust, gaslighting), isolation traps the protagonist with the threat.
- **Atmosphere precedes action.** Horror thrives on what the reader *imagines* happening. Pacing description creates dread before the jump scare; the scream before the reveal.
- **Secondary characters matter.** Horror often reveals human nature under pressure. Show how characters fracture, betray, or save one another when terrified.
- **The threat escalates.** Each encounter with horror should raise stakes or deepen understanding. Repetitive scares without progression feel padded.
- **Sensory specificity sells terror.** Cold seeping through walls, the smell of rot, tactile details of violence—specificity makes horror visceral.
- **The "why" is often secondary to the "how it feels."** Readers will accept a thin supernatural rationale if the emotional impact is overwhelming.

## Common Tropes (Use Wisely)

- **The haunted house** – Fresh if location is an active agent with history/memory; clichéd if merely a spooky setting with generic ghosts.
- **The chosen one must sacrifice** – Effective when the sacrifice costs the character emotionally or ideologically; hollow if it's a predictable plot beat.
- **The final girl/final survivor** – Works when the survivor earns escape through ingenuity or luck, not authorial fiat; avoid making it a gender-politics sop.
- **Descent into madness** – Powerful when readers doubt their own sanity alongside the character; becomes overwrought if every ambiguity is "was it real?"
- **The twist reveal** – Fresh when it recontextualizes earlier events; exhausted when the twist undermines all tension that came before.
- **The prophecy or curse** – Effective with hidden loopholes or ironic fulfillment; clichéd if straightforward or solved by exposition dump.
- **Body horror and mutation** – Potent when tied to character loss or body autonomy anxiety; gratuitous if shock value replaces thematic weight.

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)

- **Jump scares without buildup.** Effective horror sets traps; poor horror pokes the reader. Sustained tension followed by a moment of relief makes the scare land.
- **Incompetent protagonists making stupid choices.** Characters may freeze or panic, but if they ignore obvious survival tactics to maintain plot convenience, readers stop caring.
- **Graphic violence divorced from consequence.** Torture-porn excess without emotional stakes is exhausting, not frightening. Gore should unsettle, not titillate.
- **Explaining the monster to death.** Mystery and vagueness breed more dread than a detailed supernatural taxonomy. Some questions should stay unanswered.
- **Tonal whiplash.** Switching from dread to flippant humor without control undermines immersion. Dark comedy works only with precision timing.
- **False climaxes and fake-outs.** One false death is tension; three feels manipulative. Readers resent being toyed with without payoff.
- **Villain monologues instead of action.** In climactic moments, the threat should *do* something, not explain why it's evil. Show dominance through force, not words.
- **Underdeveloped secondary characters.** When supporting cast is cardboard, their deaths carry no weight, and isolation feels artificial.

## Recommended Story Structures

- **Three-Act with Escalation** – Inciting incident (threat arrives), rising action (protagonists learn and fail), climax (final confrontation or escape).
- **The Hero's Journey (with a twist)** – Protagonist crosses into the threshold of horror, undergoes trials testing their sanity and morality, returns changed (or doesn't return at all).
- **Nested Stories (Anthology Style)** – Multiple linked narratives revealing layers of a larger horror; each story raises the stakes for the next.
- **Inversion of Comfort** – Start in a familiar, safe setting; incrementally reveal that safety was illusory. The uncanny thrives on the ordinary made terrible.

## Subgenres

| Subgenre | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Cosmic Horror | Humans are insignificant; the threat is incomprehensibly vast and indifferent. Sanity-loss is central. Works by showing the observer's inadequacy (Lovecraft, modern: House of Leaves). |
| Body Horror | The human form itself is the threat: mutation, parasitism, disease. Explores bodily autonomy and loss of self. Visceral and intimate. |
| Psychological Horror | The threat may be internal (unreliable narrator, trauma) or external but filtered through fractured perception. Ambiguity and gaslighting are tools. |
| Folk Horror | Roots in local traditions, isolation, and the sinister potential of community. Often historical or rural. The village itself becomes the threat (The Wicker Man, Midsommar). |
| Gothic Horror | Atmospheric, often historical or historical-coded, emphasizing decaying architecture, family secrets, and supernatural romance. Mood over action. |
| Slasher | Focused killer(s) hunts victims in contained timeframe. Emphasis on survival and ingenuity. Often playful with conventions; can be subversive or affirmative of violence. |
| Quiet Horror | Dread without overt supernatural elements. Existential threat (illness, decay, isolation). Unsettling rather than terrifying. Works through implication. |

## Example Authors & Works

| Author | Notable Work | Known For |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Stephen King | The Shining | Building terror through isolation and psychological unraveling; humanizing the monstrous |
| Shirley Jackson | The Haunting of Hill House | Psychological atmosphere and equivocal endings; making ordinary details sinister |
| Paul Tremblay | The Cabin at the End of the World | Intimate character moments within apocalyptic dread; reframing familiar tropes |
| Laird Barron | The Cracked Spine | Cosmic horror with noir atmosphere; philosophical depth alongside visceral unease |
| Silvia Moreno-Garcia | Mexican Gothic | Atmospheric gothic with lush prose; body horror and family trauma as backdrop for supernatural threat |

## Genre-Mixing Notes

- **Horror + Fantasy:** Works when the horror emerges from the world's rules (grimdark fantasy). Fails when horror is decoration on an adventure narrative.
- **Horror + Romance:** Possible only if horror and love coexist as equal forces; vampire romance is a subgenre unto itself. Don't let romance neuter the threat.
- **Horror + Mystery:** Excellent pairing. The mystery *of the threat* drives tension forward. Whodunit + supernatural = compelling hook.
- **Horror + Science Fiction:** Works via body horror (aliens, mutation) or existential SF premises. Avoid conflating "scientifically plausible monster" with "scary."
- **Horror + Literary Fiction:** Achievable via quiet horror and psychological depth. The prose quality and thematic ambition elevate the scare.
- **Horror + Thriller:** Merges into techno-horror or psychological thriller. Must maintain visceral dread over plot momentum alone.
