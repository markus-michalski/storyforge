# Contemporary Fiction

## Overview

Contemporary fiction is set in the present day (or recent past), featuring realistic, everyday settings and recognizable modern life. The genre explores current social issues, relationships, work, identity, and the texture of now-ness. Rather than the "what if" speculation of science fiction or the magical possibilities of fantasy, contemporary fiction asks "what is it like to be alive today?" The power lies in authenticity and recognition.

## Characteristics

| Element | Convention |
|---------|-----------|
| Tone | Realistic, often conversational; can be humorous, dark, or poignant; reflects contemporary speech patterns and values |
| Pacing | Flexible; can be fast or contemplative depending on subgenre and author choice |
| POV | Any POV works; often first-person (intimate, unreliable) or close third (accessible, character-driven) |
| Typical Length | 70,000-100,000 words; length serves story needs |
| Chapter Length | 2,500-5,000 words; variable based on scene needs |
| Structure | Event-driven, character-arc-driven, or episodic; structure follows the logic of life, not necessarily neat narrative arcs |

## Key Conventions

- **Authenticity is paramount.** Readers expect recognizable contemporary settings, current technology, and realistic social dynamics. Slipping into inaccuracy breaks immersion immediately.
- **Modern concerns should be present.** Mental health, technology, social media, economic anxiety, workplace dynamics, identity politics—contemporary life includes these. Acknowledging them enriches the story.
- **Contemporary voice and language are essential.** Dialogue should sound like how people actually speak now (not stilted or artificially formal). Contemporary vernacular should feel natural.
- **Contemporary relationships vary widely.** Family structures, relationships, and identities are diverse. Representation should feel organic, not tokenistic.
- **Real problems don't have neat solutions.** Contemporary fiction often explores ambiguity and complexity without tidy resolution. This mirrors real life.
- **The ordinary can be profound.** Meaning emerges from small moments, relationships, and choices—not necessarily from plot machinery. A conversation can be as significant as an action sequence.
- **Contemporary setting enables exploration of current themes.** Authenticity in setting allows for examination of issues readers are living through.

## Common Tropes (Use Wisely)

- **The found family** – Excellent contemporary trope; characters create chosen family. Works when bonds are earned through shared experience; fails if it feels forced or sentimental.
- **The burnout or quarter-life crisis** – Resonates with contemporary readers; overdone if it's just malaise without growth or reckoning.
- **The second chance or fresh start** – Works when character actively rebuilds; fails if old patterns reassert themselves without acknowledgment.
- **The romance discovered in unlikely place** – Fresh if both characters are fully realized; tired if it's just two conventionally attractive people meeting cute.
- **The social media or technology-driven conflict** – Relevant to contemporary setting; can feel dated quickly if relied on too heavily for plot.
- **The workplace drama or professional ambition** – Excellent contemporary setting; works if work pressures realistically affect character psychology and relationships.
- **The identity crisis or self-discovery** – Central to contemporary fiction; works when exploration is genuine and growth is difficult.

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)

- **Outdated or inaccurate contemporary references.** If your book is set in 2024, don't have characters using technology or slang from five years ago. Readers notice.
- **Treating contemporary diversity as exotic.** Different family structures, sexual orientations, and racial identities are normal in contemporary fiction. They don't require explanation or apology.
- **Ignoring contemporary mental health realities.** Characters dealing with anxiety, depression, or trauma should be treated with authenticity. Avoid romanticizing mental illness.
- **Romanticizing problematic behavior.** Jealousy, possessiveness, or emotional manipulation aren't romantic in contemporary settings. Readers reject this more readily now than in older romances.
- **Contemporary voice as an excuse for sloppiness.** Modern, casual language is fine; unclear writing is not. Authentic voice doesn't mean abandoning craft.
- **Technology as plot device instead of reality.** If characters have smartphones, they should use them naturally. Forgetting they exist for plot convenience (can't call for help) feels contrived.
- **Using contemporary settings without understanding current realities.** If your story involves healthcare, education, or finances, get the details right. Readers are living these realities.
- **Treating contemporary concerns as flavor text.** If your character cares about climate change or student debt, it should affect their psychology and choices, not just be mentioned.

## Recommended Story Structures

- **The Slice-of-Life Arc** – Character navigates an ordinary season (a semester, a year, a relationship). Growth is internal and relational; no external villain or major plot event.
- **The Crisis and After** – Ordinary life is disrupted (job loss, breakup, diagnosis, loss). Character must rebuild and redefine. Recovery and adaptation are the story.
- **The Parallel Paths** – Multiple characters' stories intersect or diverge over time. Ensemble narrative showing different takes on contemporary life.
- **The Transformation Through Relationship** – Character changes through connection with another person (mentorship, friendship, romance, family). Growth is relational.

## Subgenres

| Subgenre | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Women's Fiction | Central character is a woman; concerns are often midlife transitions, identity, relationships, career. Not romance-dependent (though romance may be element). Often features female friendships and community. Predominantly female readership. |
| New Adult | Protagonist typically ages 18-30; explores the transition from adolescence to adulthood. Issues include identity, sexuality, independence, first relationships. Setting often college or post-college. |
| Slice-of-Life | Focuses on the texture of ordinary life. No major external plot; growth is internal. Emphasis on relationships and small moments. Can be quiet and contemplative. |
| Workplace Fiction | Career, workplace dynamics, professional ambition are central. Explores how work shapes identity and relationships. Can be comedy, drama, or thriller. |

|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Sally Rooney | Normal People | Contemporary Ireland; intimate character psychology; minimalist dialogue revealing complex interiority; economic and social reality; precise prose |
| Curtis Sittenfeld | Eligible | Contemporary reimagining of Jane Austen's Pride and Prejudice; modern workplace and social dynamics; sharp social observation; humor and heart |
| Celeste Ng | Our Missing Hearts | Contemporary America; family dynamics and parental love; social critique (censorship, surveillance); emotional depth; structural complexity |

## Genre-Mixing Notes

- **Contemporary + Romance:** Natural pairing. Contemporary settings provide modern relationship dynamics. Romance subplot is common or central depending on focus.
- **Contemporary + Mystery/Thriller:** Excellent combination. Present-day crime, danger, or secrets feel immediate and relevant. Both subgenres work seamlessly with contemporary setting.
- **Contemporary + Literary Fiction:** Often overlaps. Literary prose quality with contemporary authenticity deepens both elements.
- **Contemporary + Drama:** Nearly indistinguishable. Contemporary drama explores character and relationship through current settings and issues.
- **Contemporary + Comedy:** Works well; contemporary humor is readily accessible. Tone can range from light rom-com to dark comedy.
- **Contemporary + Science Fiction:** Rare but viable (near-future SF can feel contemporary; speculative contemporary fiction). Technology and speculative elements should feel grounded in recognizable now.
