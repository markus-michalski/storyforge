# LGBTQ+

## Overview

LGBTQ+ is not a standalone genre but a cross-cutting modifier that integrates LGBTQ+ characters, relationships, and themes into any base genre (romance, fantasy, horror, thriller, etc.). A queer story can be a mystery, a paranormal romance, a high-fantasy epic, or a literary coming-of-age—the difference is the inclusion of LGBTQ+ characters and the exploration of identity, belonging, and love that emerge from that perspective. The key is authenticity: queer characters should have agency, complexity, and dimensions beyond their identity.

## Key Conventions

- **Queer characters are fully realized people.** Being LGBTQ+ is part of their identity, not their entire identity. They should have goals, flaws, loves, and dreams beyond their sexuality or gender identity.
- **Identity exploration can be central, but coming-out narratives shouldn't be the only queer story.** Many LGBTQ+ characters come out in adolescence; many others navigate that earlier or later. Adults and characters who came out long ago deserve stories too.
- **Representation should reflect diversity within LGBTQ+ communities.** Gender identity, sexual orientation, race, ability, class, and culture all intersect. Avoid monolithic representations.
- **Authenticity requires research and consultation.** If writing outside your own experience (writing gay characters as a straight author, writing trans characters as a cisgender author), do research, read broadly, and consider consulting sensitivity readers.
- **Queer relationships are relationships.** LGBTQ+ romance follows the same relationship dynamics as heterosexual romance: consent, communication, and mutual respect are essential. Toxic dynamics aren't suddenly okay because they're same-sex.
- **Community and found family matter.** Many queer narratives involve chosen family and community. These relationships are as important as romantic ones.
- **Historical and societal context shapes queer experience.** Depending on setting and characters' backgrounds, homophobia, transphobia, or discrimination may be present. Acknowledge the reality without making the story about oppression alone (unless that's the focus).

## Common Tropes (Use Wisely)

- **Coming-out narrative** – Powerful when genuine and grounded in character psychology; falls flat when it's the only queer story or when it's treated as the end of the journey rather than a transition.
- **Found family/queer chosen family** – Excellent trope; works when bonds are earned and the family provides genuine support and complexity (not all conflict-free).
- **First love/first relationship** – Works when the emotional journey is the story; tired if it's reduced to "discovering sexuality" without character depth.
- **Secret relationship or closeted protagonist** – Effective when the internal conflict is genuine and the story explores what secrecy costs; falls flat if it's melodramatic without cause.
- **The wise queer mentor** – Can work but risks being a stock character. Give the mentor complexity, flaws, and their own arc.
- **Fated mates or soulmates** – Common in paranormal/fantasy romance; works when characters actively choose each other alongside destiny.
- **Conversion or redemption through love** – Use with caution. A character accepting their identity is fine; a queer person being "converted" to straight is conversion therapy rhetoric and harmful.

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)

- **Queer characters as decoration or representation checkboxes.** If a character is queer but the narrative doesn't engage with their identity or relationships, they're underdeveloped. Either commit to the character or don't include them.
- **Stereotypical or caricatured queer characters.** Avoid reducing queer identity to mannerisms or fashion. Real people are complex.
- **Resolving queer identity crises through romance alone.** Identity and love are connected but separate. A character can find themselves without finding a partner, and vice versa.
- **Treating LGBTQ+ relationships as inherently transgressive or dramatic.** Some queer relationships are ordinary, stable, and boring in the best ways. Not every queer story is a tragedy.
- **Dead queer characters or tragic endings.** While queer characters can face tragedy, patterns of killing off queer characters (Bury Your Gays trope) feel punitive. If a queer character dies, ensure it serves the story, not just diversity quota.
- **Misgendering or deadnaming characters.** Even in third-person, respect characters' pronouns and names. Using a deadname or wrong pronouns might be historically accurate but should serve narrative purpose, not be careless.
- **Forcing cishetero relationship dynamics onto queer couples.** Queer relationships don't follow straight relationship templates. Avoid imposing traditional gender roles or power dynamics.
- **Writing queer characters without consultation if outside your experience.** Research is good; consultation with people within the community you're representing is better. Avoid stereotypes and misconceptions.

## Recommended Story Structures

- **The Coming-of-Age Journey** – Character discovers and explores sexual orientation or gender identity, navigates community, finds connection and self-acceptance.
- **The Relationship Arc** – Two characters meet, connection deepens, obstacles force intimacy and understanding, relationship is earned. (Works for any genre pairing—romance, paranormal, etc.)
- **The Community Integration** – Character joins or integrates into a queer community (school community, paranormal community, found family). Belonging and identity develop through relationships.
- **The External Quest with Internal Journey** – Adventure, mystery, or external conflict serves as backdrop; character's internal identity journey parallels the external plot (common in fantasy/SF).

## Subgenres / Integration Models

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| M/M Romance | Romance between two men. Largest LGBTQ+ subgenre by publishing volume. Can range from contemporary to paranormal to fantasy. Readers often predominantly women; respect characterization beyond audience demographics. |
| F/F Romance | Romance between two women. Growing subgenre; wide variety of settings and character types. Deserves equal representation and authenticity as M/M. |
| Queer Fantasy | Fantasy with LGBTQ+ protagonists and relationships. Can be epic fantasy, paranormal, or any subgenre. Queerness should integrate with worldbuilding naturally. |
| Queer Horror | Horror with LGBTQ+ protagonists. Explores fear through queer lens (body horror may intersect with dysphoria; isolation may relate to marginalization; monsters may symbolize societal hatred). |
| Trans Narratives | Stories centering trans characters' experiences, identity, and journeys. Can be any genre. Authenticity requires research and consultation. Avoid centering cisgender characters' reactions to transness. |

## Genre-Mixing Notes

- **LGBTQ+ + Any Genre Works:** The modification applies across genres. Queer romance, queer fantasy, queer horror, queer thriller—all are viable and valid.
- **M/M + Traditional Romance Tropes:** Many tropes work (enemies-to-lovers, forced proximity, faked dating). However, avoid imposing heterosexual dynamics (one partner as "masculine," one as "feminine") unless characters explicitly identify that way.
- **F/F + Paranormal/Supernatural:** Strong pairing. Vampire romance, werewolf fiction, and supernatural romance all feature F/F relationships increasingly. Both plots can be equally weighted.
- **LGBTQ+ + Historical Fiction:** Can work but requires attention to historical accuracy. Depending on period, queer relationships may have faced suppression or danger. Acknowledge historical context without reducing story to oppression.
- **LGBTQ+ + Literary Fiction:** Excellent pairing. Literary depth with authentic representation of identity and relationships. Prose quality and thematic exploration elevate LGBTQ+ narrative.
- **Queer + Fantasy/SF Worldbuilding:** Opportunity to imagine worlds where LGBTQ+ people face different or no oppression. Alternatively, create detailed speculative societies with various attitudes toward gender/sexuality. Worldbuilding should reflect LGBTQ+ normality rather than otherness.
